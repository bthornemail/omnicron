In [geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry), a **point reflection** (also called a **point inversion** or **central inversion**) is a [geometric transformation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometric_transformation "Geometric transformation") of [affine space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Affine_space "Affine space") in which every [point](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Point_\(geometry\) "Point (geometry)") is reflected across a designated **inversion center**, which remains [fixed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fixed_point_\(mathematics\) "Fixed point (mathematics)"). In [Euclidean](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_space "Euclidean space") or [pseudo-Euclidean spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pseudo-Euclidean_space "Pseudo-Euclidean space"), a point reflection is an [isometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isometry "Isometry") (preserves [distance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_distance "Euclidean distance")).[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Point_reflection#cite_note-1) In the [Euclidean plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_plane "Euclidean plane"), a point reflection is the same as a [half-turn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Half_turn "Half turn") [rotation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotation "Rotation") (180° or π [radians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radian "Radian")), while in three-dimensional Euclidean space a point reflection is an [improper rotation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Improper_rotation "Improper rotation") which preserves distances but [reverses orientation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orientation-reversing "Orientation-reversing"). A point reflection is an [involution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Involution_\(mathematics\) "Involution (mathematics)"): applying it twice is the [identity transformation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Identity_transformation "Identity transformation").

An object that is invariant under a point reflection is said to possess **point symmetry** (also called **inversion symmetry** or **central symmetry**). A [point group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Point_group "Point group") including a point reflection among its symmetries is called _[centrosymmetric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centrosymmetry "Centrosymmetry")_. Inversion symmetry is found in many [crystal structures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crystal_structure "Crystal structure") and [molecules](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecule "Molecule"), and has a major effect upon their physical properties.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Point_reflection#cite_note-:0-2)

## Terminology

The term _reflection_ is loose, and considered by some an abuse of language, with _inversion_ preferred; however, _point reflection_ is widely used. Such maps are [involutions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Involution_\(mathematics\) "Involution (mathematics)"), meaning that they have order 2 – they are their own inverse: applying them twice yields the [identity map](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Identity_function "Identity function") – which is also true of other maps called _reflections_. More narrowly, a _[reflection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reflection_\(linear_algebra\) "Reflection (linear algebra)")_ refers to a reflection in a [hyperplane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperplane "Hyperplane") (n−1![{\displaystyle n-1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fbd0b0f32b28f51962943ee9ede4fb34198a2521) dimensional [affine subspace](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Affine_subspace "Affine subspace") – a point on the [line](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_\(geometry\) "Line (geometry)"), a line in the [plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plane_\(geometry\) "Plane (geometry)"), a plane in 3-space), with the hyperplane being fixed, but more broadly _reflection_ is applied to any involution of Euclidean space, and the fixed set (an affine space of dimension _k_, where 1≤k≤n−1![{\displaystyle 1\leq k\leq n-1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8f9adcd3d085311183b8f0dff7ec086c26e82b14)) is called the _mirror_. In dimension 1 these coincide, as a point is a hyperplane in the line.

In terms of linear algebra, assuming the origin is fixed, involutions are exactly the [diagonalizable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diagonalizable "Diagonalizable") maps with all [eigenvalues](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eigenvalue "Eigenvalue") either 1 or −1. Reflection in a hyperplane has a single −1 eigenvalue (and multiplicity n−1![{\displaystyle n-1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fbd0b0f32b28f51962943ee9ede4fb34198a2521) on the 1 eigenvalue), while point reflection has only the −1 eigenvalue (with multiplicity _n_).

The term _inversion_ should not be confused with [inversive geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inversive_geometry "Inversive geometry"), where _inversion_ is defined with respect to a circle.

In two dimensions, a point reflection is the same as a [rotation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotation "Rotation") of 180 degrees. In three dimensions, a point reflection can be described as a 180-degree rotation [composed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Composition_of_functions "Composition of functions") with reflection across the plane of rotation, perpendicular to the axis of rotation. In dimension _n_, point reflections are [orientation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orientation_\(mathematics\) "Orientation (mathematics)")-preserving if _n_ is even, and orientation-reversing if _n_ is odd.

## Formula

Given a vector **a** in the Euclidean space **R**_n_, the formula for the reflection of **a** across the point **p** is

Refp(a)=2p−a.![{\displaystyle \mathrm {Ref} _{\mathbf {p} }(\mathbf {a} )=2\mathbf {p} -\mathbf {a} .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0d26d96b4003306d34f2d9b19ec2b2e0e36f13e8)

In the case where **p** is the origin, point reflection is simply the negation of the vector **a**.

In [Euclidean geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_geometry "Euclidean geometry"), the **inversion** of a [point](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Point_\(geometry\) "Point (geometry)") _X_ with respect to a point _P_ is a point _X_* such that _P_ is the midpoint of the [line segment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_segment "Line segment") with endpoints _X_ and _X_*. In other words, the [vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_\(geometric\) "Vector (geometric)") from _X_ to _P_ is the same as the vector from _P_ to _X_*.

The formula for the inversion in _P_ is

**x*** = 2**p** − **x**

where **p**, **x** and **x*** are the position vectors of _P_, _X_ and _X_* respectively.

This [mapping](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_\(mathematics\) "Function (mathematics)") is an [isometric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isometry "Isometry") [involutive](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Involution_\(mathematics\) "Involution (mathematics)") [affine transformation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Affine_transformation "Affine transformation") which has exactly one [fixed point](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fixed_point_\(mathematics\) "Fixed point (mathematics)"), which is _P_.

## Point reflection as a special case of uniform scaling or homothety

When the inversion point _P_ coincides with the origin, point reflection is equivalent to a special case of [uniform scaling](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uniform_scaling "Uniform scaling"): uniform scaling with scale factor equal to −1. This is an example of [linear transformation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_transformation "Linear transformation").

When _P_ does not coincide with the origin, point reflection is equivalent to a special case of [homothetic transformation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homothetic_transformation "Homothetic transformation"): homothety with [homothetic center](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homothetic_center "Homothetic center") coinciding with P, and scale factor −1. (This is an example of non-linear [affine transformation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Affine_transformation "Affine transformation").)

## Point reflection group 

[![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/4/4d/Composing_Point_Reflections.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Composing_Point_Reflections.png)

The composition of two offset point reflections in 2-dimensions is a translation.

The [composition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Composition_of_functions "Composition of functions") of two point reflections is a [translation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Translation_\(geometry\) "Translation (geometry)").[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Point_reflection#cite_note-3) Specifically, point reflection at **p** followed by point reflection at **q** is translation by the vector 2(**q** − **p**).

The set consisting of all point reflections and translations is [Lie subgroup](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lie_subgroup "Lie subgroup") of the [Euclidean group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_group "Euclidean group"). It is a [semidirect product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semidirect_product "Semidirect product") of **R**_n_ with a [cyclic group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyclic_group "Cyclic group") of order 2, the latter acting on **R**_n_ by negation. It is precisely the subgroup of the Euclidean group that fixes the [line at infinity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_at_infinity "Line at infinity") pointwise.

In the case _n_ = 1, the point reflection group is the full [isometry group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_group "Euclidean group") of the line.

## Point reflections in mathematics

- Point reflection across the center of a sphere yields the [antipodal map](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antipodal_map "Antipodal map").
- A [symmetric space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemannian_symmetric_space "Riemannian symmetric space") is a [Riemannian manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemannian_manifold "Riemannian manifold") with an isometric reflection across each point. Symmetric spaces play an important role in the study of [Lie groups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lie_group "Lie group") and [Riemannian geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemannian_geometry "Riemannian geometry").

## Properties

In even-dimensional [Euclidean space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_space "Euclidean space"), say 2_N_-dimensional space, the inversion in a point _P_ is equivalent to _N_ rotations over angles π in each plane of an arbitrary set of _N_ mutually orthogonal planes intersecting at _P_. These rotations are mutually commutative. Therefore, inversion in a point in even-dimensional space is an orientation-preserving isometry or [direct isometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_group "Euclidean group").

In odd-dimensional [Euclidean space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_space "Euclidean space"), say (2_N_ + 1)-dimensional space, it is equivalent to _N_ rotations over π in each plane of an arbitrary set of _N_ mutually orthogonal planes intersecting at _P_, combined with the reflection in the 2_N_-dimensional subspace spanned by these rotation planes. Therefore, it _reverses_ rather than preserves [orientation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orientation_\(mathematics\) "Orientation (mathematics)"), it is an [indirect isometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_group "Euclidean group").

Geometrically in 3D it amounts to [rotation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotation "Rotation") about an axis through _P_ by an angle of 180°, combined with reflection in the plane through _P_ which is perpendicular to the axis; the result does not depend on the [orientation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orientation_\(rigid_body\) "Orientation (rigid body)") (in the other sense) of the axis. Notations for the type of operation, or the type of group it generates, are 1¯![{\displaystyle {\overline {1}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f50d1053ea4c96ed679d1deca6105c442b54bf11), _C__i_, _S_2, and 1×. The group type is one of the three [symmetry group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symmetry_group "Symmetry group") types in 3D without any pure [rotational symmetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotational_symmetry "Rotational symmetry"), see [cyclic symmetries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyclic_symmetries "Cyclic symmetries") with _n_ = 1.

The following [point groups in three dimensions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Point_groups_in_three_dimensions "Point groups in three dimensions") contain inversion:

- _C__n_h and _D__n_h for even _n_
- _S_2_n_ and _D__n_d for odd _n_
- _T_h, _O_h, and _I_h

Closely related to inverse in a point is [reflection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reflection_\(mathematics\) "Reflection (mathematics)") in respect to a [plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plane_\(geometry\) "Plane (geometry)"), which can be thought of as an "inversion in a plane".

## Inversion centers in crystals and molecules

Inversion symmetry plays a major role in the properties of materials, as also do other symmetry operations.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Point_reflection#cite_note-:0-2)

Some molecules contain an inversion center when a point exists through which all atoms can reflect while retaining symmetry. In many cases they can be considered as polyhedra, categorized by their coordination number and bond angles. For example, four-coordinate polyhedra are classified as [tetrahedra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetrahedral_molecular_geometry "Tetrahedral molecular geometry"), while five-coordinate environments can be [square pyramidal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square_pyramidal_molecular_geometry "Square pyramidal molecular geometry") or [trigonal bipyramidal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trigonal_bipyramidal_molecular_geometry "Trigonal bipyramidal molecular geometry") depending on the bonding angles. Six-coordinate octahedra are an example of centrosymmetric polyhedra, as the central atom acts as an inversion center through which the six bonded atoms retain symmetry. Tetrahedra, on the other hand, are non-centrosymmetric as an inversion through the central atom would result in a reversal of the polyhedron. Polyhedra with an odd (versus even) coordination number are not centrosymmtric. Polyhedra containing inversion centers are known as centrosymmetric, while those without are non-centrosymmetric. The presence or absence of an inversion center has a strong influence on the optical properties;[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Point_reflection#cite_note-4) for instance molecules without inversion symmetry have a [dipole moment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dipole_moments_of_molecules "Dipole moments of molecules") and can directly interact with photons, while those with inversion have no dipole moment and only interact via [Raman scattering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Raman_scattering "Raman scattering").[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Point_reflection#cite_note-raman1928-5) The later is named after [C. V. Raman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/C._V._Raman "C. V. Raman") who was awarded the 1930 [Nobel Prize in Physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_Physics "Nobel Prize in Physics") for his discovery.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Point_reflection#cite_note-6)

In addition, in [crystallography](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crystallography "Crystallography"), the presence of inversion centers for periodic structures distinguishes between [centrosymmetric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centrosymmetric "Centrosymmetric") and non-centrosymmetric compounds. All crystalline compounds come from a repetition of an atomic building block known as a unit cell, and these unit cells define which polyhedra form and in what order. In many materials such as oxides these polyhedra can link together via corner-, edge- or face sharing, depending on which atoms share common bonds and also the valence. In other cases such as for [metals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metals "Metals") and [alloys](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alloys "Alloys") the structures are better considered as arrangements of close-packed atoms. Crystals which do not have inversion symmetry also display the [piezoelectric effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Piezoelectric_effect "Piezoelectric effect"). The presence or absence of inversion symmetry also has numerous consequences for the properties of solids,[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Point_reflection#cite_note-:0-2) as does the mathematical relationships between the different crystal symmetries.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Point_reflection#cite_note-7)

Real polyhedra in crystals often lack the uniformity anticipated in their bonding geometry. Common irregularities found in crystallography include distortions and disorder. Distortion involves the warping of polyhedra due to nonuniform bonding lengths, often due to differing electrostatic interactions between heteroatoms or electronic effects such as [Jahn–Teller distortions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jahn%E2%80%93Teller_effect "Jahn–Teller effect"). For instance, a titanium center will likely bond evenly to six oxygens in an octahedra, but distortion would occur if one of the oxygens were replaced with a more [electronegative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronegative "Electronegative") fluorine. Distortions will not change the inherent geometry of the polyhedra—a distorted octahedron is still classified as an octahedron, but strong enough distortions can have an effect on the centrosymmetry of a compound. Disorder involves a split occupancy over two or more sites, in which an atom will occupy one crystallographic position in a certain percentage of polyhedra and the other in the remaining positions. Disorder can influence the centrosymmetry of certain polyhedra as well, depending on whether or not the occupancy is split over an already-present inversion center.

Centrosymmetry applies to the crystal structure as a whole, not just individual polyhedra. Crystals are classified into thirty-two [crystallographic point groups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crystallographic_point_groups "Crystallographic point groups") which describe how the different polyhedra arrange themselves in space in the bulk structure. Of these thirty-two point groups, eleven are centrosymmetric. The presence of noncentrosymmetric polyhedra does not guarantee that the point group will be the same—two non-centrosymmetric shapes can be oriented in space in a manner which contains an inversion center between the two. Two tetrahedra facing each other can have an inversion center in the middle, because the orientation allows for each atom to have a reflected pair. The inverse is also true, as multiple centrosymmetric polyhedra can be arranged to form a noncentrosymmetric point group.

## Inversion with respect to the origin

Inversion with respect to the origin corresponds to [additive inversion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Additive_inverse "Additive inverse") of the position vector, and also to [scalar multiplication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar_multiplication "Scalar multiplication") by −1. The operation commutes with every other [linear transformation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_transformation "Linear transformation"), but not with [translation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Translation_\(geometry\) "Translation (geometry)"): it is in the [center](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Center_\(group_theory\) "Center (group theory)") of the [general linear group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_linear_group "General linear group"). "Inversion" without indicating "in a point", "in a line" or "in a plane", means this inversion; in physics 3-dimensional reflection through the origin is also called a [parity transformation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parity_\(physics\) "Parity (physics)").

In mathematics, **reflection through the origin** refers to the point reflection of [Euclidean space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_space "Euclidean space") **R**_n_ across the [origin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Origin_\(mathematics\) "Origin (mathematics)") of the [Cartesian coordinate system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinate_system "Cartesian coordinate system"). Reflection through the origin is an [orthogonal transformation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthogonal_transformation "Orthogonal transformation") corresponding to [scalar multiplication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar_multiplication "Scalar multiplication") by −1![{\displaystyle -1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/704fb0427140d054dd267925495e78164fee9aac), and can also be written as −I![{\displaystyle -I}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1af1f812fd44b9f513f3c163c2bcc5f3f738f98d), where I![{\displaystyle I}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/535ea7fc4134a31cbe2251d9d3511374bc41be9f) is the [identity matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Identity_matrix "Identity matrix"). In three dimensions, this sends (x,y,z)↦(−x,−y,−z)![{\displaystyle (x,y,z)\mapsto (-x,-y,-z)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/58621041fdf3a9a1f65c92e16495e596fe76dc38), and so forth.

### Representations

As a [scalar matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar_matrix "Scalar matrix"), it is represented in every basis by a matrix with −1![{\displaystyle -1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/704fb0427140d054dd267925495e78164fee9aac) on the diagonal, and, together with the identity, is the [center](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Center_\(group_theory\) "Center (group theory)") of the [orthogonal group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthogonal_group "Orthogonal group") O(n)![{\displaystyle O(n)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/34109fe397fdcff370079185bfdb65826cb5565a).

It is a product of _n_ orthogonal reflections (reflection through the axes of any [orthogonal basis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthogonal_basis "Orthogonal basis")); note that orthogonal reflections commute.

In 2 dimensions, it is in fact rotation by 180 degrees, and in dimension 2n![{\displaystyle 2n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/134afa8ff09fdddd24b06f289e92e3a045092bd1), it is rotation by 180 degrees in _n_ orthogonal planes;[[a]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Point_reflection#cite_note-8) note again that rotations in orthogonal planes commute.

### Properties

It has determinant (−1)n![{\displaystyle (-1)^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c490525b94310eb9d66c0282f8d28f652af9f40a) (from the representation by a matrix or as a product of reflections). Thus it is orientation-preserving in even dimension, thus an element of the [special orthogonal group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Special_orthogonal_group "Special orthogonal group") SO(2_n_), and it is orientation-reversing in odd dimension, thus not an element of SO(2_n_ + 1) and instead providing a [splitting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Split_short_exact_sequence "Split short exact sequence") of the map O(2n+1)→±1![{\displaystyle O(2n+1)\to \pm 1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f575dc00d48b77a10948ba9ffc7d4b985ddcfa9d), showing that O(2n+1)=SO(2n+1)×{±I}![{\displaystyle O(2n+1)=SO(2n+1)\times \{\pm I\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/abcfde7bef4e9a321580949ef7d719072735590e) as an [internal direct product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Internal_direct_product "Internal direct product").

- Together with the identity, it forms the [center](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Center_\(group_theory\) "Center (group theory)") of the [orthogonal group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthogonal_group "Orthogonal group").
- It preserves every quadratic form, meaning Q(−v)=Q(v)![{\displaystyle Q(-v)=Q(v)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/aeca5d23c18bd20859f9805fb79b43c52ff0cb66), and thus is an element of every [indefinite orthogonal group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indefinite_orthogonal_group "Indefinite orthogonal group") as well.
- It equals the identity if and only if the characteristic is 2.
- It is the [longest element](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Longest_element_of_a_Coxeter_group "Longest element of a Coxeter group") of the [Coxeter group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coxeter_group "Coxeter group") of [signed permutations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Signed_permutations "Signed permutations").

Analogously, it is a longest element of the orthogonal group, with respect to the generating set of reflections: elements of the orthogonal group all have [length](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Length_function "Length function") at most _n_ with respect to the generating set of reflections,[[b]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Point_reflection#cite_note-9) and reflection through the origin has length _n,_ though it is not unique in this: other maximal combinations of rotations (and possibly reflections) also have maximal length.

### Geometry

In SO(2_r_), reflection through the origin is the farthest point from the identity element with respect to the usual metric. In O(2_r_ + 1), reflection through the origin is not in SO(2_r_+1) (it is in the non-identity component), and there is no natural sense in which it is a "farther point" than any other point in the non-identity component, but it does provide a [base point](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Base_point "Base point") in the other component.

# Geometrical optics

**Geometrical optics**, or **ray optics**, is a model of [optics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Optics "Optics") that describes [light](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light "Light") [propagation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wave_propagation "Wave propagation") in terms of _[rays](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ray_\(optics\) "Ray (optics)")_. The ray in geometrical optics is an [abstraction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abstract_object "Abstract object") useful for approximating the paths along which light propagates under certain circumstances.

The simplifying assumptions of geometrical optics include that light rays:

- propagate in straight-line paths as they travel in a [homogeneous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homogeneity_\(physics\) "Homogeneity (physics)") medium
- bend, and in particular circumstances may split in two, at the [interface](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interface_\(matter\) "Interface (matter)") between two dissimilar [media](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Optical_medium "Optical medium")
- follow curved paths in a medium in which the [refractive index](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Refractive_index "Refractive index") changes
- may be absorbed or reflected.

Geometrical optics does not account for certain optical effects such as [diffraction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diffraction "Diffraction") and [interference](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interference_\(wave_propagation\) "Interference (wave propagation)"), which are considered in [physical optics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physical_optics "Physical optics"). This simplification is useful in practice; it is an excellent approximation when the wavelength is small compared to the size of structures with which the light interacts. The techniques are particularly useful in describing geometrical aspects of [imaging](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Image "Image"), including [optical aberrations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Optical_aberration "Optical aberration").

## Explanation

Further information: [Ray (optics)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ray_\(optics\) "Ray (optics)") and [Ray tracing (physics)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ray_tracing_\(physics\) "Ray tracing (physics)")

[![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/20/Plane_wave_wavefronts_3D.svg/250px-Plane_wave_wavefronts_3D.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Plane_wave_wavefronts_3D.svg)

As light travels through space, it [oscillates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oscillation "Oscillation") in [amplitude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amplitude "Amplitude"). In this image, each maximum amplitude [crest](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crest_\(physics\) "Crest (physics)") is marked with a [plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plane_\(geometry\) "Plane (geometry)") to illustrate the [wavefront](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wavefront "Wavefront"). The [ray](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ray_\(optics\) "Ray (optics)") is the arrow [perpendicular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perpendicular "Perpendicular") to these [parallel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallel_\(geometry\) "Parallel (geometry)") surfaces.

A light ray is a [line](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_\(geometry\) "Line (geometry)") or [curve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curve "Curve") that is [perpendicular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perpendicular "Perpendicular") to the light's [wavefronts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wavefront "Wavefront") (and is therefore [collinear](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/collinear "wiktionary:collinear") with the [wave vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wave_vector "Wave vector")). A slightly more rigorous definition of a light ray follows from [Fermat's principle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fermat%27s_principle "Fermat's principle"), which states that the path taken between two points by a ray of light is the path that can be traversed in the least time.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometrical_optics#cite_note-1)

Geometrical optics is often simplified by making the [paraxial approximation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paraxial_approximation "Paraxial approximation"), or "small angle approximation". The mathematical behavior then becomes [linear](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_system "Linear system"), allowing optical components and systems to be described by simple matrices. This leads to the techniques of [Gaussian optics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gaussian_optics "Gaussian optics") and _paraxial [ray tracing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ray_tracing_\(physics\) "Ray tracing (physics)")_, which are used to find basic properties of optical systems, such as approximate [image](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Image "Image") and object positions and [magnifications](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnification "Magnification").[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometrical_optics#cite_note-Greivenkamp-2)

## Reflection

Main article: [Reflection (physics)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reflection_\(physics\) "Reflection (physics)")

[![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/Reflection_angles.svg/250px-Reflection_angles.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Reflection_angles.svg)

Diagram of [specular reflection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Specular_reflection "Specular reflection")

Glossy surfaces such as [mirrors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mirror "Mirror") reflect light in a simple, predictable way. This allows for production of reflected images that can be associated with an actual ([real](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_image "Real image")) or extrapolated ([virtual](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Virtual_image "Virtual image")) location in space.

With such surfaces, the direction of the reflected ray is determined by the angle the incident ray makes with the [surface normal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surface_normal "Surface normal"), a line perpendicular to the surface at the point where the ray hits. The incident and reflected rays lie in a single plane, and the angle between the reflected ray and the surface normal is the same as that between the incident ray and the normal.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometrical_optics#cite_note-Geoptics-3) This is known as the [Law of Reflection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Law_of_Reflection "Law of Reflection").

For [flat mirrors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plane_mirror "Plane mirror"), the law of reflection implies that images of objects are upright and the same distance behind the mirror as the objects are in front of the mirror. The image size is the same as the object size. (The [magnification](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnification "Magnification") of a flat mirror is equal to one.) The law also implies that [mirror images](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mirror_image "Mirror image") are [parity inverted](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parity_\(physics\) "Parity (physics)"), which is perceived as a left-right inversion.

[Mirrors with curved surfaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curved_mirror "Curved mirror") can be modeled by [ray tracing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ray_tracing_\(physics\) "Ray tracing (physics)") and using the law of reflection at each point on the surface. For [mirrors with parabolic surfaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parabolic_reflector "Parabolic reflector"), parallel rays incident on the mirror produce reflected rays that converge at a common [focus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Focus_\(optics\) "Focus (optics)"). Other curved surfaces may also focus light, but with aberrations due to the diverging shape causing the focus to be smeared out in space. In particular, spherical mirrors exhibit [spherical aberration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spherical_aberration "Spherical aberration"). Curved mirrors can form images with magnification greater than or less than one, and the image can be upright or inverted. An upright image formed by reflection in a mirror is always virtual, while an inverted image is real and can be projected onto a screen.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometrical_optics#cite_note-Geoptics-3)

# Reflection coefficient

In [physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics "Physics") and [electrical engineering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrical_engineering "Electrical engineering") the **reflection coefficient** is a parameter that describes how much of a wave is reflected by an impedance discontinuity in the transmission medium. It is equal to the ratio of the [amplitude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amplitude "Amplitude") of the reflected wave to the incident wave, with each expressed as [phasors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phasor "Phasor"). For example, it is used in [optics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Optics "Optics") to calculate the amount of light that is reflected from a surface with a different index of refraction, such as a glass surface, or in an electrical [transmission line](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transmission_line "Transmission line") to calculate how much of the [electromagnetic wave](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reflections_of_signals_on_conducting_lines "Reflections of signals on conducting lines") is reflected by an impedance discontinuity. The reflection coefficient is closely related to the _[transmission coefficient](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transmission_coefficient "Transmission coefficient")_. The [reflectance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reflectance "Reflectance") of a system is also sometimes called a reflection coefficient.

[![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/3/30/Partial_transmittance.gif)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Partial_transmittance.gif)

A wave is partially transmitted and partially reflected when the medium through which it travels suddenly changes. The reflection coefficient determines the ratio of the reflected wave amplitude to the incident wave amplitude.

Different disciplines have different applications for the term.

## Transmission lines

See also: [Reflections of signals on conducting lines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reflections_of_signals_on_conducting_lines "Reflections of signals on conducting lines") and [Signal reflection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Signal_reflection "Signal reflection")

In [telecommunications](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Telecommunications "Telecommunications") and [transmission line](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transmission_line "Transmission line") theory, the reflection coefficient is the [ratio](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ratio "Ratio") of the [complex amplitude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_amplitude "Complex amplitude") of the reflected wave to that of the incident wave. The voltage and current at any point along a transmission line can always be resolved into forward and reflected traveling waves given a specified reference impedance _Z0_. The reference impedance used is typically the [characteristic impedance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Characteristic_impedance "Characteristic impedance") of a transmission line that's involved, but one can speak of reflection coefficient without any actual transmission line being present. In terms of the forward and reflected waves determined by the voltage and current, the reflection coefficient is defined as the [complex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_number "Complex number") ratio of the voltage of the reflected wave (V−![{\displaystyle V^{-}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9fa9ed53745ca8c9a03fbbb6c28c341c067aaa0f)) to that of the incident wave (V+![{\displaystyle V^{+}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4b2aafc830b7719ab820d87c8a8eec52e8756b9c)). This is typically represented with a Γ![{\displaystyle \Gamma }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4cfde86a3f7ec967af9955d0988592f0693d2b19) (capital [gamma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gamma "Gamma")) and can be written as:

Γ=V−V+![{\displaystyle \Gamma ={\frac {V^{-}}{V^{+}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/33eb5c95e58a153a5ad7de947135819e4184ee23)

It can also be defined using the _currents_ associated with the reflected and forward waves, but introducing a minus sign to account for the opposite orientations of the two currents:

Γ=−I−I+=V−V+![{\displaystyle \Gamma =-{\frac {I^{-}}{I^{+}}}={\frac {V^{-}}{V^{+}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2fb3f9b8bcf7f91283b62e88b63c25bd2cb4022a)

The reflection coefficient may also be established using other field or [circuit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronic_circuit "Electronic circuit") pairs of quantities whose product defines power resolvable into a forward and reverse wave. With electromagnetic plane waves, one uses the ratio of the electric fields of the reflected to that of the incident wave (or magnetic fields, again with a minus sign); the ratio of each wave's electric field _E_ to its magnetic field _H_ is the medium's characteristic impedance, Z0![{\displaystyle Z_{0}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bfcd49ab63d30163ac54e60a8e24ff9ccd7bcd44), (equal to the [impedance of free space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Impedance_of_free_space "Impedance of free space") if the medium is a vacuum).[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reflection_coefficient#cite_note-1)

[![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/Reflection_Coefficient_Circuit.svg/250px-Reflection_Coefficient_Circuit.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Reflection_Coefficient_Circuit.svg)

Simple circuit configuration showing measurement location of reflection coefficient

In the accompanying figure, a signal source with internal impedance ZS![{\displaystyle Z_{S}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f35299f14001027f7c1494d579f8f8a3120e8666) possibly followed by a transmission line of characteristic impedance ZS![{\displaystyle Z_{S}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f35299f14001027f7c1494d579f8f8a3120e8666) is represented by its [Thévenin equivalent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Th%C3%A9venin_equivalent "Thévenin equivalent"), driving the load ZL![{\displaystyle Z_{L}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8fb811eeda6b543aafa2f13aa8bbeb80fd77a0c2). For a real (resistive) source impedance ZS![{\displaystyle Z_{S}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f35299f14001027f7c1494d579f8f8a3120e8666), if we define Γ![{\displaystyle \Gamma }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4cfde86a3f7ec967af9955d0988592f0693d2b19) using the reference impedance Z0=ZS![{\displaystyle Z_{0}=Z_{S}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a0a87f625599e6938f5fccd86c6d25d7a941c147) then the source's [maximum power is delivered](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Impedance_matching#Reflection-less_matching "Impedance matching") to a load ZL=Z0![{\displaystyle Z_{L}=Z_{0}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0e4e1bcfa469e843e07359c395467b7be795a3c2), in which case Γ=0![{\displaystyle \Gamma =0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f9896b8ebf4dd4079563aefd15653a9880687480) implying no reflected power. More generally, the squared-magnitude of the reflection coefficient |Γ|2![{\displaystyle |\Gamma |^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1878b988d2e4dafbb71e4ab635051a612dba36fa) denotes the proportion of that power that is reflected back to the source, with the power actually delivered toward the load being 1−|Γ|2![{\displaystyle 1-|\Gamma |^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/dd7e19643c5065373b0eb7fd891c9077559f3f05).

Anywhere along an intervening (lossless) transmission line of characteristic impedance Z0![{\displaystyle Z_{0}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bfcd49ab63d30163ac54e60a8e24ff9ccd7bcd44), the magnitude of the reflection coefficient |Γ|![{\displaystyle |\Gamma |}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0c417bf8f8d99da4c7f0f82ce92f6ba665fffb22) will remain the same (the powers of the forward and reflected waves stay the same) but with a different phase. In the case of a short circuited load (ZL=0![{\displaystyle Z_{L}=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5bc00cba1a332143d271849472562697f0fc980e)), one finds Γ=−1![{\displaystyle \Gamma =-1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5a5b71df0d3d176c3b99ddbd40ecd749fc247aa9) at the load. This implies the reflected wave having a 180° phase shift (phase reversal) with the voltages of the two waves being opposite at that point and adding to zero (as a short circuit demands).

### Relation to load impedance

The reflection coefficient is determined by the load impedance at the end of the transmission line, as well as the [characteristic impedance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Characteristic_impedance "Characteristic impedance") of the line. A load impedance of ZL![{\displaystyle Z_{L}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8fb811eeda6b543aafa2f13aa8bbeb80fd77a0c2) terminating a line with a characteristic impedance of Z0![{\displaystyle Z_{0}\,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d21b2afe643f3f5f93e6bcfb280a92d34339d9d9) will have a reflection coefficient of

Γ=ZL−Z0ZL+Z0.![{\displaystyle \Gamma ={Z_{L}-Z_{0} \over Z_{L}+Z_{0}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0b2c1eb05aab3011af7ca6923fd719357b1b2db1)

This is the coefficient at the load. The reflection coefficient can also be measured at other points on the line. The _magnitude_ of the reflection coefficient in a lossless transmission line is constant along the line (as are the powers in the forward and reflected waves). However its _phase_ will be shifted by an amount dependent on the [electrical distance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrical_length "Electrical length") ϕ![{\displaystyle \phi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/72b1f30316670aee6270a28334bdf4f5072cdde4) from the load. If the coefficient is measured at a point L![{\displaystyle L}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/103168b86f781fe6e9a4a87b8ea1cebe0ad4ede8) meters from the load, so the [electrical distance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrical_length "Electrical length") from the load is ϕ=2πL/λ![{\displaystyle \phi =2\pi L/\lambda }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cde8bd52d1a570006f3737088dfd5329555bb8ff) radians, the coefficient Γ′![{\displaystyle \Gamma '}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cc1c460d7474b68828ae8281cad517b61348df7a) at that point will be

Γ′=Γe−i2ϕ![{\displaystyle \Gamma '=\Gamma e^{-i\,2\phi }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e6dd66c336f1880f236f355d00aea81ddd32e0be)

Note that the phase of the reflection coefficient is changed by _twice_ the phase length of the attached transmission line. That is to take into account not only the phase delay of the reflected wave, but the phase shift that had first been applied to the forward wave, with the reflection coefficient being the quotient of these. The reflection coefficient so measured, Γ′![{\displaystyle \Gamma '}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cc1c460d7474b68828ae8281cad517b61348df7a), corresponds to an impedance which is generally dissimilar to ZL![{\displaystyle Z_{L}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8fb811eeda6b543aafa2f13aa8bbeb80fd77a0c2) present at the far side of the transmission line.

The complex reflection coefficient (in the region |Γ|≤1![{\displaystyle |\Gamma |\leq 1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c57049754fc4cd30f4a176be4bc248ae843a38a1), corresponding to passive loads) may be displayed graphically using a [Smith chart](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Smith_chart "Smith chart"). The Smith chart is a polar plot of Γ![{\displaystyle \Gamma }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4cfde86a3f7ec967af9955d0988592f0693d2b19), therefore the magnitude of Γ![{\displaystyle \Gamma }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4cfde86a3f7ec967af9955d0988592f0693d2b19) is given directly by the distance of a point to the center (with the edge of the Smith chart corresponding to |Γ|=1![{\displaystyle |\Gamma |=1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f35a0b80be6e7e0c631f7112ef6740b4009c0423)). Its evolution along a transmission line is likewise described by a rotation of 2ϕ![{\displaystyle 2\phi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/913feff4f1627646eea7f2a496a94ffee3c1c11b) around the chart's center. Using the scales on a Smith chart, the resulting impedance (normalized to Z0![{\displaystyle Z_{0}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bfcd49ab63d30163ac54e60a8e24ff9ccd7bcd44)) can directly be read. Before the advent of modern electronic computers, the Smith chart was of particular use as a sort of [analog computer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nomogram "Nomogram") for this purpose.

The reflected power in terms of the reflection coefficient is:

Preflected=Pincident|Γ|2![{\displaystyle P_{reflected}=P_{incident}|\Gamma |^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bbfd3695d13480fc43a887e1cbf106d2011acdb6) .

### Standing wave ratio

Main article: [Standing wave ratio](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standing_wave_ratio "Standing wave ratio")

The [standing wave ratio](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standing_wave_ratio "Standing wave ratio") (SWR) is determined solely by the _magnitude_ of the reflection coefficient:

SWR=1+|Γ|1−|Γ|.![{\displaystyle SWR={1+|\Gamma | \over 1-|\Gamma |}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3c9ac17652556b677e8a727ef7db280411520e8a)

Along a lossless transmission line of characteristic impedance _Z_0, the SWR signifies the ratio of the voltage (or current) maxima to minima (or what it would be if the transmission line were long enough to produce them). The above calculation assumes that Γ![{\displaystyle \Gamma }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4cfde86a3f7ec967af9955d0988592f0693d2b19) has been calculated using _Z_0 as the reference impedance. Since it uses only the _magnitude_ of Γ![{\displaystyle \Gamma }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4cfde86a3f7ec967af9955d0988592f0693d2b19), the SWR intentionally ignores the specific value of the load impedance _ZL_ responsible for it, but only the magnitude of the resulting [impedance mismatch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Impedance_mismatch "Impedance mismatch"). That SWR remains the same wherever measured along a transmission line (looking towards the load) since the addition of a transmission line length to a load ZL![{\displaystyle Z_{L}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8fb811eeda6b543aafa2f13aa8bbeb80fd77a0c2) only changes the phase, not magnitude of Γ![{\displaystyle \Gamma }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4cfde86a3f7ec967af9955d0988592f0693d2b19). While having a one-to-one correspondence with reflection coefficient, SWR is the most commonly used figure of merit in describing the mismatch affecting a [radio antenna](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radio_antenna "Radio antenna") or antenna system. It is most often [measured](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SWR_meter "SWR meter") at the transmitter side of a transmission line, but having, as explained, the same value as would be measured at the antenna (load) itself.

## Electrical networks

A transmission line is an example of a [2-port](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Two-port_network "Two-port network") [electrical network](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrical_network "Electrical network"), but reflection coefficients are useful in the analysis of any electrical networks. A reflection coefficient for each port in the same way as for the boundary of a transmission line. It will, however, also depend on the properties of connections at other ports and so is not a property intrinsic to the network itself. For a 2-port network with the 2x2 [scattering matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scattering_parameters "Scattering parameters") _S_, and with a source and load connected to its input and output, where the reflections off the source back into the input are ΓS![{\displaystyle \Gamma _{S}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/974863df213a910130845c98a9f45b4521ca1437) and the reflections off the load back into the output are ΓL![{\displaystyle \Gamma _{L}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/eb9e1cecff63c9ffb5dd2fb41603122b2ca9d89c), then the reflection coefficients at the input and output are given by:[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reflection_coefficient#cite_note-2)

## Optics and microwaves

Main article: [Fresnel equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fresnel_equations "Fresnel equations")

In [optics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Optics "Optics") and electromagnetics in general, _reflection coefficient_ can refer to either the amplitude reflection coefficient described here, or the [reflectance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reflectance "Reflectance"), depending on context. Typically, the reflectance is represented by a capital _R_, while the amplitude reflection coefficient is represented by a lower-case _r_. These related concepts are covered by [Fresnel equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fresnel_equations "Fresnel equations") in [classical optics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Optics#Classical_optics "Optics").

## Acoustics

Main article: [Acoustic wave § Reflection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acoustic_wave#Reflection "Acoustic wave")

Acousticians use reflection coefficients to understand the effect of different materials on their acoustic environments. The field properties used to define the reflection coefficient are typically the acoustic pressure and velocity in the incident and reflected [acoustic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acoustics "Acoustics") waves.
