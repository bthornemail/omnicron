From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

Linear transform from the time domain to the frequency domain

Not to be confused with [Fisher z-transformation](/wiki/Fisher_z-transformation "Fisher z-transformation").

In [mathematics](/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics") and [signal processing](/wiki/Signal_processing "Signal processing"), the **Z-transform** converts a [discrete-time signal](/wiki/Discrete-time_signal "Discrete-time signal"), which is a [sequence](/wiki/Sequence "Sequence") of [real](/wiki/Real_number "Real number") or [complex numbers](/wiki/Complex_number "Complex number"), into a complex valued [frequency-domain](/wiki/Frequency-domain "Frequency-domain") (the **z-domain** or **z-plane**) representation.[\[1\]](#cite_note-Mandal_2020_pp._157–195-1)[\[2\]](#cite_note-Lynn_1986_pp._225–272-2)[\[3\]](#cite_note-JuryBook-3)

It can be considered a discrete-time counterpart of the [Laplace transform](/wiki/Laplace_transform "Laplace transform") (the _s-domain_ or _s-plane_).[\[4\]](#cite_note-Palani_pp._921–1055-4) This similarity is explored in the theory of [time-scale calculus](/wiki/Time-scale_calculus "Time-scale calculus").

While the [continuous-time Fourier transform](/wiki/Continuous-time_Fourier_transform "Continuous-time Fourier transform") is evaluated on the s-domain's vertical axis (the imaginary axis), the [discrete-time Fourier transform](/wiki/Discrete-time_Fourier_transform "Discrete-time Fourier transform") is evaluated along the z-domain's [unit circle](/wiki/Unit_circle "Unit circle"). The s-domain's left [half-plane](/wiki/Half-plane "Half-plane") maps to the area inside the z-domain's unit circle, while the s-domain's right half-plane maps to the area outside of the z-domain's unit circle.

In signal processing, one of the means of designing [digital filters](/wiki/Digital_filter "Digital filter") is to take analog designs, subject them to a [bilinear transform](/wiki/Bilinear_transform "Bilinear transform") which maps them from the s-domain to the z-domain, and then produce the digital filter by inspection, manipulation, or numerical approximation. Such methods tend not to be accurate except in the vicinity of the complex unity, i.e. at low frequencies.

## History

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Z-transform&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: History")\]

The basic idea now known as the Z-transform was known to [Laplace](/wiki/Laplace "Laplace"), and it was re-introduced in 1947 by [W. Hurewicz](/wiki/Witold_Hurewicz "Witold Hurewicz")[\[5\]](#cite_note-kanasewich-5)[\[6\]](#cite_note-6) and others as a way to treat sampled-data control systems used with radar. It gives a tractable way to solve linear, constant-coefficient [difference equations](/wiki/Difference_equation "Difference equation"). It was later dubbed "the z-transform" by [Ragazzini](/wiki/John_R._Ragazzini "John R. Ragazzini") and [Zadeh](/wiki/Lotfi_A._Zadeh "Lotfi A. Zadeh") in the sampled-data control group at Columbia University in 1952.[\[7\]](#cite_note-7)[\[8\]](#cite_note-8)

The modified or [advanced Z-transform](/wiki/Advanced_Z-transform "Advanced Z-transform") was later developed and popularized by [E. I. Jury](/wiki/Eliahu_I._Jury "Eliahu I. Jury").[\[9\]](#cite_note-9)[\[10\]](#cite_note-10)

The idea contained within the Z-transform is also known in mathematical literature as the method of [generating functions](/wiki/Generating_function "Generating function") which can be traced back as early as 1730 when it was introduced by [de Moivre](/wiki/Abraham_de_Moivre "Abraham de Moivre") in conjunction with probability theory.[\[11\]](#cite_note-11) From a mathematical view the Z-transform can also be viewed as a [Laurent series](/wiki/Laurent_series "Laurent series") where one views the sequence of numbers under consideration as the (Laurent) expansion of an analytic function.

## Definition

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Z-transform&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Definition")\]

The Z-transform can be defined as either a _one-sided_ or _two-sided_ transform (similarly to the [one-sided Laplace transform](/wiki/Laplace_transform "Laplace transform") and the [two-sided Laplace transform](/wiki/Two-sided_Laplace_transform "Two-sided Laplace transform")).[\[12\]](#cite_note-Jackson_1996_pp._29–54-12)

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Z-transform&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Bilateral Z-transform")\]

### Unilateral Z-transform

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Z-transform&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Unilateral Z-transform")\]

Alternatively, in cases where x \[ n \] {\\displaystyle x\[n\]} ![{\displaystyle x[n]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/864cbbefbdcb55af4d9390911de1bf70167c4a3d) is defined only for ⁠ n ≥ 0 {\\displaystyle n\\geq 0} ![{\displaystyle n\geq 0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ce8a1b7b3bc3c790054d93629fc3b08cd1da1fd0) ⁠, the _single-sided_ or _unilateral_ Z-transform is defined as:

X ( z ) \= Z { x \[ n \] } \= ∑ n \= 0 ∞ x \[ n \] z − n . {\\displaystyle X(z)={\\mathcal {Z}}\\{x\[n\]\\}=\\sum \_{n=0}^{\\infty }x\[n\]z^{-n}.} ![{\displaystyle X(z)={\mathcal {Z}}\{x[n]\}=\sum _{n=0}^{\infty }x[n]z^{-n}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a3e560ddcffcbab6fa176f4d2dd8e3fe60905b55) 

In [signal processing](/wiki/Signal_processing "Signal processing"), this definition can be used to evaluate the Z-transform of the [unit impulse response](/wiki/Finite_impulse_response#Frequency_response "Finite impulse response") of a discrete-time [causal system](/wiki/Causal_system "Causal system").

An important example of the unilateral Z-transform is the [probability-generating function](/wiki/Probability-generating_function "Probability-generating function"), where the component x \[ n \] {\\displaystyle x\[n\]} ![{\displaystyle x[n]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/864cbbefbdcb55af4d9390911de1bf70167c4a3d) is the probability that a discrete random variable takes the value ⁠ n {\\displaystyle n} ![{\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b) ⁠. The properties of Z-transforms (listed in _[§ Properties](#Properties)_) have useful interpretations in the context of probability theory.

## Inverse Z-transform

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Z-transform&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Inverse Z-transform")\]

The _inverse_ Z-transform is:

x \[ n \] \= Z − 1 { X ( z ) } \= 1 2 π i ∮ C X ( z ) z n − 1 d z {\\displaystyle x\[n\]={\\mathcal {Z}}^{-1}\\{X(z)\\}={\\frac {1}{2\\pi i}}\\oint \_{C}X(z)z^{n-1}dz} ![{\displaystyle x[n]={\mathcal {Z}}^{-1}\{X(z)\}={\frac {1}{2\pi i}}\oint _{C}X(z)z^{n-1}dz}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4847f4c62386ec4a5ae39a5051b2e49d1a544ffb) 

where C {\\displaystyle C} ![{\displaystyle C}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4fc55753007cd3c18576f7933f6f089196732029) is a counterclockwise closed path encircling the origin and entirely in the [region of convergence](/wiki/Radius_of_convergence "Radius of convergence") (ROC). In the case where the ROC is causal (see [Example 2](#Example_2_\(causal_ROC\))), this means the path C {\\displaystyle C} ![{\displaystyle C}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4fc55753007cd3c18576f7933f6f089196732029) must encircle all of the poles of ⁠ X ( z ) {\\displaystyle X(z)} ![{\displaystyle X(z)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/727fb275ca22820bf91e526120c4939a1d38a2b0) ⁠.

A special case of this [contour integral](/wiki/Contour_integral "Contour integral") occurs when C {\\displaystyle C} ![{\displaystyle C}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4fc55753007cd3c18576f7933f6f089196732029) is the unit circle. This contour can be used when the ROC includes the unit circle, which is always guaranteed when X ( z ) {\\displaystyle X(z)} ![{\displaystyle X(z)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/727fb275ca22820bf91e526120c4939a1d38a2b0) is stable, that is, when all the poles are inside the unit circle. With this contour, the inverse Z-transform simplifies to the [inverse discrete-time Fourier transform](/wiki/Discrete-time_Fourier_transform#Inverse_transform "Discrete-time Fourier transform"), or [Fourier series](/wiki/Fourier_series "Fourier series"), of the periodic values of the Z-transform around the unit circle:

x \[ n \] \= 1 2 π ∫ − π π X ( e i ω ) e i ω n d ω . {\\displaystyle x\[n\]={\\frac {1}{2\\pi }}\\int \_{-\\pi }^{\\pi }X(e^{i\\omega })e^{i\\omega n}d\\omega .} ![{\displaystyle x[n]={\frac {1}{2\pi }}\int _{-\pi }^{\pi }X(e^{i\omega })e^{i\omega n}d\omega .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7abd1e49840a65aaf87198484575e78a29896399) 

The Z-transform with a finite range of n {\\displaystyle n} ![{\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b) and a finite number of uniformly spaced z {\\displaystyle z} ![{\displaystyle z}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bf368e72c009decd9b6686ee84a375632e11de98) values can be computed efficiently via [Bluestein's FFT algorithm](/wiki/Bluestein%27s_FFT_algorithm "Bluestein's FFT algorithm"). The [discrete-time Fourier transform](/wiki/Discrete-time_Fourier_transform "Discrete-time Fourier transform") (DTFT)—not to be confused with the [discrete Fourier transform](/wiki/Discrete_Fourier_transform "Discrete Fourier transform") (DFT)—is a special case of such a Z-transform obtained by restricting z {\\displaystyle z} ![{\displaystyle z}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bf368e72c009decd9b6686ee84a375632e11de98) to lie on the unit circle.

The following three methods are often used for the evaluation of the inverse -transform,

### Direct evaluation by contour integration

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Z-transform&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Direct evaluation by contour integration")\]

This method involves applying the [Cauchy Residue Theorem](/wiki/Residue_theorem "Residue theorem") to evaluate the inverse Z-transform. By integrating around a closed contour in the complex plane, the residues at the poles of the Z-transform function inside the ROC are summed. This technique is particularly useful when working with functions expressed in terms of complex variables.

### Expansion into a series of terms in the variables _z_ and _z_−1

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Z-transform&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Expansion into a series of terms in the variables z and z−1")\]

In this method, the Z-transform is expanded into a power series. This approach is useful when the Z-transform function is rational, allowing for the approximation of the inverse by expanding into a series and determining the signal coefficients term by term.

### Partial-fraction expansion and table lookup

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Z-transform&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: Partial-fraction expansion and table lookup")\]

This technique decomposes the Z-transform into a sum of simpler fractions, each corresponding to known Z-transform pairs. The inverse Z-transform is then determined by looking up each term in a standard table of Z-transform pairs. This method is widely used for its efficiency and simplicity, especially when the original function can be easily broken down into recognizable components.

## Region of convergence

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Z-transform&action=edit&section=10 "Edit section: Region of convergence")\]

See also: [Pole–zero plot § Discrete-time systems](/wiki/Pole%E2%80%93zero_plot#Discrete-time_systems "Pole–zero plot")

The [region of convergence](/wiki/Radius_of_convergence "Radius of convergence") (ROC) is the set of points in the complex plane for which the Z-transform summation [absolutely converges](/wiki/Absolute_convergence "Absolute convergence"):

R O C \= { z : ∑ n \= − ∞ ∞ | x \[ n \] z − n | < ∞ } {\\displaystyle \\mathrm {ROC} =\\left\\{z:\\sum \_{n=-\\infty }^{\\infty }\\left|x\[n\]z^{-n}\\right|<\\infty \\right\\}} ![{\displaystyle \mathrm {ROC} =\left\{z:\sum _{n=-\infty }^{\infty }\left|x[n]z^{-n}\right|<\infty \right\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/be526fbdde2155d84128328df2240b5e403794ab)

### Examples conclusion

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Z-transform&action=edit&section=14 "Edit section: Examples conclusion")\]

Examples 2 and 3 clearly show that the Z-transform X ( z ) {\\displaystyle X(z)} ![{\displaystyle X(z)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/727fb275ca22820bf91e526120c4939a1d38a2b0) of x \[ n \] {\\displaystyle x\[n\]} ![{\displaystyle x[n]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/864cbbefbdcb55af4d9390911de1bf70167c4a3d) is unique when and only when specifying the ROC. Creating the [pole–zero plot](/wiki/Pole%E2%80%93zero_plot "Pole–zero plot") for the causal and anticausal case show that the ROC for either case does not include the pole that is at ⁠ 0.5 {\\displaystyle 0.5} ![{\displaystyle 0.5}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c867fe7d5d53ce2c0790852289b794c6ed185f36) ⁠. This extends to cases with multiple poles: the ROC will _never_ contain poles.

In example 2, the causal system yields a ROC that includes | z | \= ∞ {\\displaystyle |z|=\\infty } ![{\displaystyle |z|=\infty }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8a7264daab866deaa5ed3737f4bd28e20c1c0b5f) while the anticausal system in example 3 yields an ROC that includes ⁠ | z | \= 0 {\\displaystyle \\vert z\\vert =0} ![{\displaystyle \vert z\vert =0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7c8405e37568535137f8da1bb6722b7bfccbb506) ⁠.

![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/Region_of_convergence_0.5_0.75_mixed-causal.svg/250px-Region_of_convergence_0.5_0.75_mixed-causal.svg.png)
ROC shown as a blue ring 0.5 < |z| < 0.75

In systems with multiple poles it is possible to have a ROC that includes neither | z | \= ∞ {\\displaystyle |z|=\\infty } ![{\displaystyle |z|=\infty }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8a7264daab866deaa5ed3737f4bd28e20c1c0b5f) nor ⁠ | z | \= 0 {\\displaystyle \\vert z\\vert =0} ![{\displaystyle \vert z\vert =0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7c8405e37568535137f8da1bb6722b7bfccbb506) ⁠. The ROC creates a circular band. For example,

x \[ n \] \= ( 0.5 ) n u \[ n \] − ( 0.75 ) n u \[ − n − 1 \] {\\displaystyle x\[n\]=(0.5)^{n}\\,u\[n\]-(0.75)^{n}\\,u\[-n-1\]} ![{\displaystyle x[n]=(0.5)^{n}\,u[n]-(0.75)^{n}\,u[-n-1]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/24757e0ebe39ff24fbadb18b584dd3e4844c47dc) 

has poles at ⁠ 0.5 {\\displaystyle 0.5} ![{\displaystyle 0.5}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c867fe7d5d53ce2c0790852289b794c6ed185f36) ⁠ and ⁠ 0.75 {\\displaystyle 0.75} ![{\displaystyle 0.75}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/26172b039ecff22e5dce5ea1ab643e36581cbd29) ⁠. The ROC will be ⁠ 0.5 < | z | < 0.75 {\\displaystyle 0.5<\\vert z\\vert <0.75} ![{\displaystyle 0.5<\vert z\vert <0.75}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c086710f5058affc1f89456fa3c84d37cef77674) ⁠, which includes neither the origin nor infinity. Such a system is called a mixed-causality system as it contains a causal term ( 0.5 ) n u \[ n \] {\\displaystyle (0.5)^{n}\\,u\[n\]} ![{\displaystyle (0.5)^{n}\,u[n]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/40d19ef089110f0eece4b4a90c5e0642f045feba) and an anticausal term ⁠ − ( 0.75 ) n u \[ − n − 1 \] {\\displaystyle -(0.75)^{n}\\,u\[-n-1\]} ![{\displaystyle -(0.75)^{n}\,u[-n-1]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9450c39589570ab6d88dac329978b6673e2610d3) ⁠.

The [stability](/wiki/Control_theory#Stability "Control theory") of a system can also be determined by knowing the ROC alone. If the ROC contains the unit circle (i.e., ⁠ | z | \= 1 {\\displaystyle \\vert z\\vert =1} ![{\displaystyle \vert z\vert =1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8bb84bad9f9e29adcbedeb591693f83c1b303476) ⁠) then the system is stable. In the above systems the causal system (Example 2) is stable because ⁠ | z | \> 0.5 {\\displaystyle \\vert z\\vert >0.5} ![{\displaystyle \vert z\vert >0.5}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4cd4b53ba4813d833be9b09e5ae7decab136f9c3) ⁠ contains the unit circle.

Let us assume we are provided a Z-transform of a system without a ROC (i.e., an ambiguous ⁠ x \[ n \] {\\displaystyle x\[n\]} ![{\displaystyle x[n]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/864cbbefbdcb55af4d9390911de1bf70167c4a3d) ⁠). We can determine a unique x \[ n \] {\\displaystyle x\[n\]} ![{\displaystyle x[n]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/864cbbefbdcb55af4d9390911de1bf70167c4a3d) provided we desire the following:

-   Stability
-   Causality

For stability the ROC must contain the unit circle. If we need a causal system then the ROC must contain infinity and the system function will be a right-sided sequence. If we need an anticausal system then the ROC must contain the origin and the system function will be a left-sided sequence. If we need both stability and causality, all the poles of the system function must be inside the unit circle.

The unique x \[ n \] {\\displaystyle x\[n\]} ![{\displaystyle x[n]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/864cbbefbdcb55af4d9390911de1bf70167c4a3d) can then be found.

### Bilinear transform

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Z-transform&action=edit&section=19 "Edit section: Bilinear transform")\]

Main article: [Bilinear transform](/wiki/Bilinear_transform "Bilinear transform")

The **bilinear transform** can be used to convert continuous-time filters (represented in the Laplace domain) into discrete-time filters (represented in the Z-domain), and vice versa. The following substitution is used:

s \= 2 T ( z − 1 ) ( z + 1 ) {\\displaystyle s={\\frac {2}{T}}{\\frac {(z-1)}{(z+1)}}} ![{\displaystyle s={\frac {2}{T}}{\frac {(z-1)}{(z+1)}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1527916eca8cf855130fff97af602c1e40764042) 

to convert some function H ( s ) {\\displaystyle H(s)} ![{\displaystyle H(s)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f1b91390324fc9c33ec00fe57e3924ad7118cc1e) in the Laplace domain to a function H ( z ) {\\displaystyle H(z)} ![{\displaystyle H(z)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b3c5a542a7eaa29c58fb64cbeb5133ce98ac4f4b) in the Z-domain ([Tustin transformation](/wiki/Bilinear_transform "Bilinear transform")), or

z \= e s T ≈ 1 + s T / 2 1 − s T / 2 {\\displaystyle z=e^{sT}\\approx {\\frac {1+sT/2}{1-sT/2}}} ![{\displaystyle z=e^{sT}\approx {\frac {1+sT/2}{1-sT/2}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/62a6580b6e5ae71b9fbbba9c99b583769f38219f) 

from the Z-domain to the Laplace domain. Through the bilinear transformation, the complex _s_\-plane (of the Laplace transform) is mapped to the complex z-plane (of the z-transform). While this mapping is (necessarily) nonlinear, it is useful in that it maps the entire i ω {\\displaystyle i\\omega } ![{\displaystyle i\omega }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/23fc9e7cff2cec46693f08dbcc2908d9964be18e) axis of the _s_\-plane onto the [unit circle](/wiki/Unit_circle "Unit circle") in the z-plane. As such, the Fourier transform (which is the Laplace transform evaluated on the i ω {\\displaystyle i\\omega } ![{\displaystyle i\omega }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/23fc9e7cff2cec46693f08dbcc2908d9964be18e) axis) becomes the discrete-time Fourier transform. This assumes that the Fourier transform exists; i.e., that the i ω {\\displaystyle i\\omega } ![{\displaystyle i\omega }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/23fc9e7cff2cec46693f08dbcc2908d9964be18e) axis is in the region of convergence of the Laplace transform.

## Linear constant-coefficient difference equation

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Z-transform&action=edit&section=21 "Edit section: Linear constant-coefficient difference equation")\]

The linear constant-coefficient difference (LCCD) equation is a representation for a linear system based on the [autoregressive moving-average](/wiki/Autoregressive_moving_average_model "Autoregressive moving average model") equation:

∑ p \= 0 N y \[ n − p \] α p \= ∑ q \= 0 M x \[ n − q \] β q . {\\displaystyle \\sum \_{p=0}^{N}y\[n-p\]\\alpha \_{p}=\\sum \_{q=0}^{M}x\[n-q\]\\beta \_{q}.} ![{\displaystyle \sum _{p=0}^{N}y[n-p]\alpha _{p}=\sum _{q=0}^{M}x[n-q]\beta _{q}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f5fb60ed165f7ce5a60a48d0451fd542cc95f468) 

Both sides of the above equation can be divided by α 0 {\\displaystyle \\alpha \_{0}} ![{\displaystyle \alpha _{0}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a214eff2fcc322f780dd8837e7472b0edb994a13) if it is not zero. By normalizing with ⁠ α 0 \= 1 {\\displaystyle \\alpha \_{0}=1} ![{\displaystyle \alpha _{0}=1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5bd274fa4181e1040ceca1281de46985bb81f903) ⁠, the LCCD equation can be written

y \[ n \] \= ∑ q \= 0 M x \[ n − q \] β q − ∑ p \= 1 N y \[ n − p \] α p . {\\displaystyle y\[n\]=\\sum \_{q=0}^{M}x\[n-q\]\\beta \_{q}-\\sum \_{p=1}^{N}y\[n-p\]\\alpha \_{p}.} ![{\displaystyle y[n]=\sum _{q=0}^{M}x[n-q]\beta _{q}-\sum _{p=1}^{N}y[n-p]\alpha _{p}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fea05d580f1b1fecb9f9aa3ec3d80a9e0d8b02b1) 

This form of the LCCD equation is favorable to make it more explicit that the "current" output y \[ n \] {\\displaystyle y\[n\]} ![{\displaystyle y[n]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/305428e6d1fb59cd0163a7a96ace52292a262afa) is a function of past outputs ⁠ y \[ n − p \] {\\displaystyle y\[n-p\]} ![{\displaystyle y[n-p]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c09ed15524c7d70b8d4ffa35fe8a5eba4bb7f8a5) ⁠, current input ⁠ x \[ n \] {\\displaystyle x\[n\]} ![{\displaystyle x[n]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/864cbbefbdcb55af4d9390911de1bf70167c4a3d) ⁠, and previous inputs ⁠ x \[ n − q \] {\\displaystyle x\[n-q\]} ![{\displaystyle x[n-q]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/544e257b0dc157d83ee3453f3af32c23921d67c5) ⁠.

### Transfer function

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Z-transform&action=edit&section=22 "Edit section: Transfer function")\]

Taking the Z-transform of the above equation (using linearity and time-shifting laws) yields:

Y ( z ) ∑ p \= 0 N z − p α p \= X ( z ) ∑ q \= 0 M z − q β q {\\displaystyle Y(z)\\sum \_{p=0}^{N}z^{-p}\\alpha \_{p}=X(z)\\sum \_{q=0}^{M}z^{-q}\\beta \_{q}} ![{\displaystyle Y(z)\sum _{p=0}^{N}z^{-p}\alpha _{p}=X(z)\sum _{q=0}^{M}z^{-q}\beta _{q}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/45ca2f7943c9d0d8785b22bd1a45aad32c0dfc2b) 

where X ( z ) {\\displaystyle X(z)} ![{\displaystyle X(z)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/727fb275ca22820bf91e526120c4939a1d38a2b0) and Y ( z ) {\\displaystyle Y(z)} ![{\displaystyle Y(z)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1529e80a525ae5701df402042a02b40f0bd7d1a4) are the Z-transform of x \[ n \] {\\displaystyle x\[n\]} ![{\displaystyle x[n]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/864cbbefbdcb55af4d9390911de1bf70167c4a3d) and ⁠ y \[ n \] {\\displaystyle y\[n\]} ![{\displaystyle y[n]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/305428e6d1fb59cd0163a7a96ace52292a262afa) ⁠, respectively. (Notation conventions typically use capitalized letters to refer to the Z-transform of a signal denoted by a corresponding lower case letter, similar to the convention used for notating Laplace transforms.)

Rearranging results in the system's [transfer function](/wiki/Transfer_function "Transfer function"):

H ( z ) \= Y ( z ) X ( z ) \= ∑ q \= 0 M z − q β q ∑ p \= 0 N z − p α p \= β 0 + z − 1 β 1 + z − 2 β 2 + ⋯ + z − M β M α 0 + z − 1 α 1 + z − 2 α 2 + ⋯ + z − N α N . {\\displaystyle H(z)={\\frac {Y(z)}{X(z)}}={\\frac {\\sum \_{q=0}^{M}z^{-q}\\beta \_{q}}{\\sum \_{p=0}^{N}z^{-p}\\alpha \_{p}}}={\\frac {\\beta \_{0}+z^{-1}\\beta \_{1}+z^{-2}\\beta \_{2}+\\cdots +z^{-M}\\beta \_{M}}{\\alpha \_{0}+z^{-1}\\alpha \_{1}+z^{-2}\\alpha \_{2}+\\cdots +z^{-N}\\alpha \_{N}}}.} ![{\displaystyle H(z)={\frac {Y(z)}{X(z)}}={\frac {\sum _{q=0}^{M}z^{-q}\beta _{q}}{\sum _{p=0}^{N}z^{-p}\alpha _{p}}}={\frac {\beta _{0}+z^{-1}\beta _{1}+z^{-2}\beta _{2}+\cdots +z^{-M}\beta _{M}}{\alpha _{0}+z^{-1}\alpha _{1}+z^{-2}\alpha _{2}+\cdots +z^{-N}\alpha _{N}}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2e1493bc96427856a8b0431b05823bf1946048a4) 

### Zeros and poles

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Z-transform&action=edit&section=23 "Edit section: Zeros and poles")\]

From the [fundamental theorem of algebra](/wiki/Fundamental_theorem_of_algebra "Fundamental theorem of algebra") the [numerator](/wiki/Numerator "Numerator") has M {\\displaystyle M} ![{\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd) [roots](/wiki/Root_of_a_function "Root of a function") (corresponding to zeros of _H {\\displaystyle H} ![{\displaystyle H}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/75a9edddcca2f782014371f75dca39d7e13a9c1b)_ ) and the [denominator](/wiki/Denominator "Denominator") has N {\\displaystyle N} ![{\displaystyle N}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f5e3890c981ae85503089652feb48b191b57aae3) roots (corresponding to poles). Rewriting the [transfer function](/wiki/Transfer_function "Transfer function") in terms of [zeros and poles](/wiki/Zeros_and_poles "Zeros and poles")

H ( z ) \= ( 1 − q 1 z − 1 ) ( 1 − q 2 z − 1 ) ⋯ ( 1 − q M z − 1 ) ( 1 − p 1 z − 1 ) ( 1 − p 2 z − 1 ) ⋯ ( 1 − p N z − 1 ) , {\\displaystyle H(z)={\\frac {(1-q\_{1}z^{-1})(1-q\_{2}z^{-1})\\cdots (1-q\_{M}z^{-1})}{(1-p\_{1}z^{-1})(1-p\_{2}z^{-1})\\cdots (1-p\_{N}z^{-1})}},} ![{\displaystyle H(z)={\frac {(1-q_{1}z^{-1})(1-q_{2}z^{-1})\cdots (1-q_{M}z^{-1})}{(1-p_{1}z^{-1})(1-p_{2}z^{-1})\cdots (1-p_{N}z^{-1})}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b5179b5152f6b6bed748035043b3c21bf6c6b059) 

where q k {\\displaystyle q\_{k}} ![{\displaystyle q_{k}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f27215e46abcad60f100434d2c8003310580af95) is the k {\\displaystyle k} ![{\displaystyle k}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3c9a2c7b599b37105512c5d570edc034056dd40) th zero and p k {\\displaystyle p\_{k}} ![{\displaystyle p_{k}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/01084a31964201514f3e6bd0136989e11ea6e58a) is the k {\\displaystyle k} ![{\displaystyle k}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3c9a2c7b599b37105512c5d570edc034056dd40) th pole. The zeros and poles are commonly complex and when plotted on the complex plane (z-plane) it is called the [pole–zero plot](/wiki/Pole%E2%80%93zero_plot "Pole–zero plot").

In addition, there may also exist zeros and poles at z \= 0 {\\displaystyle z=0} ![{\displaystyle z=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b92bfc06485cc90286474b14a516a68d8bfdd7b3) and ⁠ z \= ∞ {\\displaystyle z=\\infty } ![{\displaystyle z=\infty }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ce32f3cbf3b98a02776b78bd6991867897112b4a) ⁠. If we take these poles and zeros as well as multiple-order zeros and poles into consideration, the number of zeros and poles are always equal.

By factoring the denominator, [partial fraction](/wiki/Partial_fraction "Partial fraction") decomposition can be used, which can then be transformed back to the time domain. Doing so would result in the [impulse response](/wiki/Impulse_response "Impulse response") and the linear constant coefficient difference equation of the system.

### Output response

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Z-transform&action=edit&section=24 "Edit section: Output response")\]

If such a system H ( z ) {\\displaystyle H(z)} ![{\displaystyle H(z)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b3c5a542a7eaa29c58fb64cbeb5133ce98ac4f4b) is driven by a signal X ( z ) {\\displaystyle X(z)} ![{\displaystyle X(z)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/727fb275ca22820bf91e526120c4939a1d38a2b0) then the output is ⁠ Y ( z ) \= H ( z ) X ( z ) {\\displaystyle Y(z)=H(z)X(z)} ![{\displaystyle Y(z)=H(z)X(z)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/65365e6239799a82e1bdb93cecdcb656e6a2e4f1) ⁠. By performing [partial fraction](/wiki/Partial_fraction "Partial fraction") decomposition on Y ( z ) {\\displaystyle Y(z)} ![{\displaystyle Y(z)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1529e80a525ae5701df402042a02b40f0bd7d1a4) and then taking the inverse Z-transform the output y \[ n \] {\\displaystyle y\[n\]} ![{\displaystyle y[n]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/305428e6d1fb59cd0163a7a96ace52292a262afa) can be found. In practice, it is often useful to fractionally decompose Y ( z ) z {\\displaystyle \\textstyle {\\frac {Y(z)}{z}}} ![{\displaystyle \textstyle {\frac {Y(z)}{z}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b11645e83917dbeb757146305317f4e7ca02db76) before multiplying that quantity by z {\\displaystyle z} ![{\displaystyle z}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bf368e72c009decd9b6686ee84a375632e11de98) to generate a form of Y ( z ) {\\displaystyle Y(z)} ![{\displaystyle Y(z)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1529e80a525ae5701df402042a02b40f0bd7d1a4) which has terms with easily computable inverse Z-transforms.
