In [mathematics](/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics"), a **formal series** is an infinite sum that is considered independently from any notion of [convergence](/wiki/Convergent_series "Convergent series"), and can be manipulated with the usual algebraic operations on [series](/wiki/Series_\(mathematics\) "Series (mathematics)") (addition, subtraction, multiplication, division, [partial sums](/wiki/Partial_sum "Partial sum"), etc.).

A **formal power series** is a special kind of formal series, of the form ∑ n \= 0 ∞ a n x n \= a 0 + a 1 x + a 2 x 2 + ⋯ , {\\displaystyle \\sum \_{n=0}^{\\infty }a\_{n}x^{n}=a\_{0}+a\_{1}x+a\_{2}x^{2}+\\cdots ,} ![{\displaystyle \sum _{n=0}^{\infty }a_{n}x^{n}=a_{0}+a_{1}x+a_{2}x^{2}+\cdots ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2f747fd4ec4cf0c5dda1867628ff099119e68f05) where the a n , {\\displaystyle a\_{n},} ![{\displaystyle a_{n},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/31e82abf89fa8ccdcef4b7e68944e7d27a65b60f) called _coefficients_, are numbers or, more generally, elements of some [ring](/wiki/Ring_\(mathematics\) "Ring (mathematics)"), and the x n {\\displaystyle x^{n}} ![{\displaystyle x^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/150d38e238991bc4d0689ffc9d2a852547d2658d) are formal powers of the symbol x {\\displaystyle x} ![{\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4) that is called an [indeterminate](/wiki/Indeterminate_\(variable\) "Indeterminate (variable)") or, commonly, a [variable](/wiki/Variable_\(mathematics\) "Variable (mathematics)"). Hence, formal power series can be viewed as a generalization of [polynomials](/wiki/Polynomial "Polynomial") where the number of terms is allowed to be infinite, and differ from usual [power series](/wiki/Power_series "Power series") by the absence of convergence requirements, which implies that a formal power series may not represent a function of its variables. Formal power series are in [one to one correspondence](/wiki/One_to_one_correspondence "One to one correspondence") with their [sequences](/wiki/Sequence_\(mathematics\) "Sequence (mathematics)") of coefficients, but the two concepts must not be confused, since the operations that can be applied are different.

A formal power series with coefficients in a ring R {\\displaystyle R} ![{\displaystyle R}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4b0bfb3769bf24d80e15374dc37b0441e2616e33) is called a formal power series over R . {\\displaystyle R.} ![{\displaystyle R.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fdcae6b33a27f86c7961318cd7ee3d789d3bcdd2) The formal power series over a ring R {\\displaystyle R} ![{\displaystyle R}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4b0bfb3769bf24d80e15374dc37b0441e2616e33) form a ring, commonly denoted by R \[ \[ x \] \] . {\\displaystyle R\[\[x\]\].} ![{\displaystyle R[[x]].}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/278375a52199d29953718c9958dcbe75f572dc5d) (It can be seen as the [(_x_)\-adic completion](/wiki/I-adic_topology#Completion "I-adic topology") of the [polynomial ring](/wiki/Polynomial_ring "Polynomial ring") R \[ x \] , {\\displaystyle R\[x\],} ![{\displaystyle R[x],}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fc2b009d35a62963b7b217ea739ee218449729d9) in the same way as the [p\-adic integers](/wiki/P-adic_integer "P-adic integer") are the p\-adic completion of the ring of the integers.)

Formal powers series in several indeterminates are defined similarly by replacing the powers of a single indeterminate by [monomials](/wiki/Monomial "Monomial") in several indeterminates.

Formal power series are widely used in [combinatorics](/wiki/Combinatorics "Combinatorics") for representing sequences of integers as [generating functions](/wiki/Generating_function "Generating function"). In this context, a [recurrence relation](/wiki/Recurrence_relation "Recurrence relation") between the elements of a sequence may often be interpreted as a [differential equation](/wiki/Differential_equation "Differential equation") that the generating function satisfies. This allows using methods of [complex analysis](/wiki/Complex_analysis "Complex analysis") for combinatorial problems (see [analytic combinatorics](/wiki/Analytic_combinatorics "Analytic combinatorics")).

## Introduction

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Formal_power_series&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Introduction")\]

A formal power series can be loosely thought of as an object that is like a [polynomial](/wiki/Polynomial "Polynomial"), but with infinitely many terms. Alternatively, for those familiar with [power series](/wiki/Power_series "Power series") (or [Taylor series](/wiki/Taylor_series "Taylor series")), one may think of a formal power series as a power series in which we ignore questions of [convergence](/wiki/Convergent_series "Convergent series") by not assuming that the variable _X_ denotes any numerical value (not even an unknown value). For example, consider the series A \= 1 − 3 X + 5 X 2 − 7 X 3 + 9 X 4 − 11 X 5 + ⋯ . {\\displaystyle A=1-3X+5X^{2}-7X^{3}+9X^{4}-11X^{5}+\\cdots .} ![{\displaystyle A=1-3X+5X^{2}-7X^{3}+9X^{4}-11X^{5}+\cdots .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/84d90dbc2e766447b978dcbe337c510a5564bf24) If we studied this as a power series, its properties would include, for example, that its [radius of convergence](/wiki/Radius_of_convergence "Radius of convergence") is 1 by the [Cauchy–Hadamard theorem](/wiki/Cauchy%E2%80%93Hadamard_theorem "Cauchy–Hadamard theorem"). However, as a formal power series, we may ignore this completely; all that is relevant is the sequence of [coefficients](/wiki/Coefficient "Coefficient") \[1, −3, 5, −7, 9, −11, ...\]. In other words, a formal power series is an object that just records a sequence of coefficients. It is perfectly acceptable to consider a formal power series with the [factorials](/wiki/Factorial "Factorial") \[1, 1, 2, 6, 24, 120, 720, 5040, ... \] as coefficients, even though the corresponding power series diverges for any nonzero value of _X_.

Algebra on formal power series is carried out by simply pretending that the series are polynomials. For example, if

B \= 2 X + 4 X 3 + 6 X 5 + ⋯ , {\\displaystyle B=2X+4X^{3}+6X^{5}+\\cdots ,} ![{\displaystyle B=2X+4X^{3}+6X^{5}+\cdots ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fc26f9851b5fb188799528723912fa78739e35fe) 

then we add _A_ and _B_ term by term:

A + B \= 1 − X + 5 X 2 − 3 X 3 + 9 X 4 − 5 X 5 + ⋯ . {\\displaystyle A+B=1-X+5X^{2}-3X^{3}+9X^{4}-5X^{5}+\\cdots .} ![{\displaystyle A+B=1-X+5X^{2}-3X^{3}+9X^{4}-5X^{5}+\cdots .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/273945a2d931a6902a0b8d9a78e8c67bdb26fa1d) 

We can multiply formal power series, again just by treating them as polynomials (see in particular [Cauchy product](/wiki/Cauchy_product "Cauchy product")):

A B \= 2 X − 6 X 2 + 14 X 3 − 26 X 4 + 44 X 5 + ⋯ . {\\displaystyle AB=2X-6X^{2}+14X^{3}-26X^{4}+44X^{5}+\\cdots .} ![{\displaystyle AB=2X-6X^{2}+14X^{3}-26X^{4}+44X^{5}+\cdots .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3171f56c4ed60205ba5774d8b11f608a3b659e58) 

Notice that each coefficient in the product _AB_ only depends on a _finite_ number of coefficients of _A_ and _B_. For example, the _X_5 term is given by

44 X 5 \= ( 1 × 6 X 5 ) + ( 5 X 2 × 4 X 3 ) + ( 9 X 4 × 2 X ) . {\\displaystyle 44X^{5}=(1\\times 6X^{5})+(5X^{2}\\times 4X^{3})+(9X^{4}\\times 2X).} ![{\displaystyle 44X^{5}=(1\times 6X^{5})+(5X^{2}\times 4X^{3})+(9X^{4}\times 2X).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a49d224a5bfbc2cc4e76f09fe20599716d6ed35b) 

For this reason, one may multiply formal power series without worrying about the usual questions of [absolute](/wiki/Absolute_convergence "Absolute convergence"), [conditional](/wiki/Conditional_convergence "Conditional convergence") and [uniform convergence](/wiki/Uniform_convergence "Uniform convergence") which arise in dealing with power series in the setting of [analysis](/wiki/Mathematical_analysis "Mathematical analysis").

Once we have defined multiplication for formal power series, we can define multiplicative inverses as follows. The multiplicative inverse of a formal power series _A_ is a formal power series _C_ such that _AC_ = 1, provided that such a formal power series exists. It turns out that if _A_ has a multiplicative inverse, it is unique, and we denote it by _A_−1. Now we can define division of formal power series by defining _B_/_A_ to be the product _BA_−1, provided that the inverse of _A_ exists. For example, one can use the definition of multiplication above to verify the familiar formula

1 1 + X \= 1 − X + X 2 − X 3 + X 4 − X 5 + ⋯ . {\\displaystyle {\\frac {1}{1+X}}=1-X+X^{2}-X^{3}+X^{4}-X^{5}+\\cdots .} ![{\displaystyle {\frac {1}{1+X}}=1-X+X^{2}-X^{3}+X^{4}-X^{5}+\cdots .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bacfd1790a7aa81f5ab70dfdc759a377fe749032) 

An important operation on formal power series is coefficient extraction. In its most basic form, the coefficient extraction operator \[ X n \] {\\displaystyle \[X^{n}\]} ![{\displaystyle [X^{n}]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/badc958349e01f5300025ae5f395186006bc9ecf) applied to a formal power series A {\\displaystyle A} ![{\displaystyle A}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7daff47fa58cdfd29dc333def748ff5fa4c923e3) in one variable extracts the coefficient of the n {\\displaystyle n} ![{\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b) th power of the variable, so that \[ X 2 \] A \= 5 {\\displaystyle \[X^{2}\]A=5} ![{\displaystyle [X^{2}]A=5}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a7cc2fb6276ea1b09e7509c604a24db789f9907b) and \[ X 5 \] A \= − 11 {\\displaystyle \[X^{5}\]A=-11} ![{\displaystyle [X^{5}]A=-11}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/45ab3cad81a1aa1fd08adb13f76dbbe50900c924) . Other examples include

\[ X 3 \] ( B ) \= 4 , \[ X 2 \] ( X + 3 X 2 Y 3 + 10 Y 6 ) \= 3 Y 3 , \[ X 2 Y 3 \] ( X + 3 X 2 Y 3 + 10 Y 6 ) \= 3 , \[ X n \] ( 1 1 + X ) \= ( − 1 ) n , \[ X n \] ( X ( 1 − X ) 2 ) \= n . {\\displaystyle {\\begin{aligned}\\left\[X^{3}\\right\](B)&=4,\\\\\\left\[X^{2}\\right\](X+3X^{2}Y^{3}+10Y^{6})&=3Y^{3},\\\\\\left\[X^{2}Y^{3}\\right\](X+3X^{2}Y^{3}+10Y^{6})&=3,\\\\\\left\[X^{n}\\right\]\\left({\\frac {1}{1+X}}\\right)&=(-1)^{n},\\\\\\left\[X^{n}\\right\]\\left({\\frac {X}{(1-X)^{2}}}\\right)&=n.\\end{aligned}}} ![{\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}\left[X^{3}\right](B)&=4,\\\left[X^{2}\right](X+3X^{2}Y^{3}+10Y^{6})&=3Y^{3},\\\left[X^{2}Y^{3}\right](X+3X^{2}Y^{3}+10Y^{6})&=3,\\\left[X^{n}\right]\left({\frac {1}{1+X}}\right)&=(-1)^{n},\\\left[X^{n}\right]\left({\frac {X}{(1-X)^{2}}}\right)&=n.\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5c26a96e701e52c0b6af5953f3299eb156592213) 

Similarly, many other operations that are carried out on polynomials can be extended to the formal power series setting, as explained below.

### Definition of the formal power series ring

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Formal_power_series&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Definition of the formal power series ring")\]

One can characterize R \[ \[ X \] \] {\\displaystyle R\[\[X\]\]} ![{\displaystyle R[[X]]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fc00b68cd3f2c0346dea47d276cea5331fa55159) abstractly as the [completion](/wiki/Completeness_\(topology\) "Completeness (topology)") of the [polynomial](/wiki/Polynomial "Polynomial") ring R \[ X \] {\\displaystyle R\[X\]} ![{\displaystyle R[X]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/afeccd52deabb878398a8485755c3ceea80caf9a) equipped with a particular [metric](/wiki/Metric_space "Metric space"). This automatically gives R \[ \[ X \] \] {\\displaystyle R\[\[X\]\]} ![{\displaystyle R[[X]]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fc00b68cd3f2c0346dea47d276cea5331fa55159) the structure of a [topological ring](/wiki/Topological_ring "Topological ring") (and even of a complete metric space). But the general construction of a completion of a metric space is more involved than what is needed here, and would make formal power series seem more complicated than they are. It is possible to describe R \[ \[ X \] \] {\\displaystyle R\[\[X\]\]} ![{\displaystyle R[[X]]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fc00b68cd3f2c0346dea47d276cea5331fa55159) more explicitly, and define the ring structure and topological structure separately, as follows.

#### Ring structure

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Formal_power_series&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Ring structure")\]

As a set, R \[ \[ X \] \] {\\displaystyle R\[\[X\]\]} ![{\displaystyle R[[X]]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fc00b68cd3f2c0346dea47d276cea5331fa55159) can be constructed as the set R N {\\displaystyle R^{\\mathbb {N} }} ![{\displaystyle R^{\mathbb {N} }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0bd24305615f037911537d9378785ece88e6a248) of all infinite sequences of elements of R {\\displaystyle R} ![{\displaystyle R}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4b0bfb3769bf24d80e15374dc37b0441e2616e33) , indexed by the [natural numbers](/wiki/Natural_number "Natural number") (taken to include 0). Designating a sequence whose term at index n {\\displaystyle n} ![{\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b) is a n {\\displaystyle a\_{n}} ![{\displaystyle a_{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/790f9209748c2dca7ed7b81932c37c02af1dbc31) by ( a n ) {\\displaystyle (a\_{n})} ![{\displaystyle (a_{n})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/18bc33c7c35d82b00f88d3a9103ed4738cde41f9) , one defines addition of two such sequences by

( a n ) n ∈ N + ( b n ) n ∈ N \= ( a n + b n ) n ∈ N {\\displaystyle (a\_{n})\_{n\\in \\mathbb {N} }+(b\_{n})\_{n\\in \\mathbb {N} }=\\left(a\_{n}+b\_{n}\\right)\_{n\\in \\mathbb {N} }} ![{\displaystyle (a_{n})_{n\in \mathbb {N} }+(b_{n})_{n\in \mathbb {N} }=\left(a_{n}+b_{n}\right)_{n\in \mathbb {N} }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8882d109f1428f6376081379a696c01af48b1054) 

and multiplication by

( a n ) n ∈ N × ( b n ) n ∈ N \= ( ∑ k \= 0 n a k b n − k ) n ∈ N . {\\displaystyle (a\_{n})\_{n\\in \\mathbb {N} }\\times (b\_{n})\_{n\\in \\mathbb {N} }=\\left(\\sum \_{k=0}^{n}a\_{k}b\_{n-k}\\right)\_{\\!n\\in \\mathbb {N} }.} ![{\displaystyle (a_{n})_{n\in \mathbb {N} }\times (b_{n})_{n\in \mathbb {N} }=\left(\sum _{k=0}^{n}a_{k}b_{n-k}\right)_{\!n\in \mathbb {N} }.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87b5a7d7fb7d85a32a141910b10eb876fbc07831) 

This type of product is called the [Cauchy product](/wiki/Cauchy_product "Cauchy product") of the two sequences of coefficients, and is a sort of discrete [convolution](/wiki/Convolution "Convolution"). With these operations, R N {\\displaystyle R^{\\mathbb {N} }} ![{\displaystyle R^{\mathbb {N} }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0bd24305615f037911537d9378785ece88e6a248) becomes a commutative ring with zero element ( 0 , 0 , 0 , … ) {\\displaystyle (0,0,0,\\ldots )} ![{\displaystyle (0,0,0,\ldots )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cf8f05f7a603ace331accbac5bcfb60b6c6020e7) and multiplicative identity ( 1 , 0 , 0 , … ) {\\displaystyle (1,0,0,\\ldots )} ![{\displaystyle (1,0,0,\ldots )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e183dea3b37917abb34408113e58406c95939c4b) .

The product is in fact the same one used to define the product of polynomials in one indeterminate, which suggests using a similar notation. One embeds R {\\displaystyle R} ![{\displaystyle R}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4b0bfb3769bf24d80e15374dc37b0441e2616e33) into R \[ \[ X \] \] {\\displaystyle R\[\[X\]\]} ![{\displaystyle R[[X]]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fc00b68cd3f2c0346dea47d276cea5331fa55159) by sending any (constant) a ∈ R {\\displaystyle a\\in R} ![{\displaystyle a\in R}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/49b8332c70318b1c764cb4bcea0a6eb274600975) to the sequence ( a , 0 , 0 , … ) {\\displaystyle (a,0,0,\\ldots )} ![{\displaystyle (a,0,0,\ldots )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f6f8bf3b6f342c87f5aed432b7bf071acefa0719) and designates the sequence ( 0 , 1 , 0 , 0 , … ) {\\displaystyle (0,1,0,0,\\ldots )} ![{\displaystyle (0,1,0,0,\ldots )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/51b0e80f55c0f49a9461da853e33912fbd3a0379) by X {\\displaystyle X} ![{\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab) ; then using the above definitions every sequence with only finitely many nonzero terms can be expressed in terms of these special elements as

( a 0 , a 1 , a 2 , … , a n , 0 , 0 , … ) \= a 0 + a 1 X + ⋯ + a n X n \= ∑ i \= 0 n a i X i ; {\\displaystyle (a\_{0},a\_{1},a\_{2},\\ldots ,a\_{n},0,0,\\ldots )=a\_{0}+a\_{1}X+\\cdots +a\_{n}X^{n}=\\sum \_{i=0}^{n}a\_{i}X^{i};} ![{\displaystyle (a_{0},a_{1},a_{2},\ldots ,a_{n},0,0,\ldots )=a_{0}+a_{1}X+\cdots +a_{n}X^{n}=\sum _{i=0}^{n}a_{i}X^{i};}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3deef49b4945129c520d4769ce6dae86f78b63d0) 

these are precisely the polynomials in X {\\displaystyle X} ![{\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab) . Given this, it is quite natural and convenient to designate a general sequence ( a n ) n ∈ N {\\displaystyle (a\_{n})\_{n\\in \\mathbb {N} }} ![{\displaystyle (a_{n})_{n\in \mathbb {N} }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/70f0c27000fbc03d920b5b678949a0043ad269bd) by the formal expression ∑ i ∈ N a i X i {\\displaystyle \\textstyle \\sum \_{i\\in \\mathbb {N} }a\_{i}X^{i}} ![{\displaystyle \textstyle \sum _{i\in \mathbb {N} }a_{i}X^{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6b736d98f5c25d9f8189ff56c141c2299506584e) , even though the latter _is not_ an expression formed by the operations of addition and multiplication defined above (from which only finite sums can be constructed). This notational convention allows reformulation of the above definitions as

( ∑ i ∈ N a i X i ) + ( ∑ i ∈ N b i X i ) \= ∑ i ∈ N ( a i + b i ) X i {\\displaystyle \\left(\\sum \_{i\\in \\mathbb {N} }a\_{i}X^{i}\\right)+\\left(\\sum \_{i\\in \\mathbb {N} }b\_{i}X^{i}\\right)=\\sum \_{i\\in \\mathbb {N} }(a\_{i}+b\_{i})X^{i}} ![{\displaystyle \left(\sum _{i\in \mathbb {N} }a_{i}X^{i}\right)+\left(\sum _{i\in \mathbb {N} }b_{i}X^{i}\right)=\sum _{i\in \mathbb {N} }(a_{i}+b_{i})X^{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/710f916e2322f9ec0dc0ed2a48f0b833ecd481cf) 

and

( ∑ i ∈ N a i X i ) × ( ∑ i ∈ N b i X i ) \= ∑ n ∈ N ( ∑ k \= 0 n a k b n − k ) X n . {\\displaystyle \\left(\\sum \_{i\\in \\mathbb {N} }a\_{i}X^{i}\\right)\\times \\left(\\sum \_{i\\in \\mathbb {N} }b\_{i}X^{i}\\right)=\\sum \_{n\\in \\mathbb {N} }\\left(\\sum \_{k=0}^{n}a\_{k}b\_{n-k}\\right)X^{n}.} ![{\displaystyle \left(\sum _{i\in \mathbb {N} }a_{i}X^{i}\right)\times \left(\sum _{i\in \mathbb {N} }b_{i}X^{i}\right)=\sum _{n\in \mathbb {N} }\left(\sum _{k=0}^{n}a_{k}b_{n-k}\right)X^{n}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e1a07f830bad00dc6cbfa9ae2774dace3c42032b) 

which is quite convenient, but one must be aware of the distinction between formal summation (a mere convention) and actual addition.

#### Topological structure

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Formal_power_series&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Topological structure")\]

Having stipulated conventionally that

( a 0 , a 1 , a 2 , a 3 , … ) \= ∑ i \= 0 ∞ a i X i , {\\displaystyle (a\_{0},a\_{1},a\_{2},a\_{3},\\ldots )=\\sum \_{i=0}^{\\infty }a\_{i}X^{i},} ![{\displaystyle (a_{0},a_{1},a_{2},a_{3},\ldots )=\sum _{i=0}^{\infty }a_{i}X^{i},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/46e4ee62a7e090613f1fd2fa206e27d2f2755eb8) 

1

one would like to interpret the right hand side as a well-defined infinite summation. To that end, a notion of convergence in R N {\\displaystyle R^{\\mathbb {N} }} ![{\displaystyle R^{\mathbb {N} }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0bd24305615f037911537d9378785ece88e6a248) is defined and a [topology](/wiki/Topology "Topology") on R N {\\displaystyle R^{\\mathbb {N} }} ![{\displaystyle R^{\mathbb {N} }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0bd24305615f037911537d9378785ece88e6a248) is constructed. There are several equivalent ways to define the desired topology.

-   We may give R N {\\displaystyle R^{\\mathbb {N} }} ![{\displaystyle R^{\mathbb {N} }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0bd24305615f037911537d9378785ece88e6a248) the [product topology](/wiki/Product_topology "Product topology"), where each copy of R {\\displaystyle R} ![{\displaystyle R}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4b0bfb3769bf24d80e15374dc37b0441e2616e33) is given the [discrete topology](/wiki/Discrete_topology "Discrete topology").
-   We may give R N {\\displaystyle R^{\\mathbb {N} }} ![{\displaystyle R^{\mathbb {N} }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0bd24305615f037911537d9378785ece88e6a248) the [I-adic topology](/wiki/I-adic_topology "I-adic topology"), where I \= ( X ) {\\displaystyle I=(X)} ![{\displaystyle I=(X)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/65a8f33c9ca43b355c4d930f58793d877630d895) is the ideal generated by X {\\displaystyle X} ![{\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab) , which consists of all sequences whose first term a 0 {\\displaystyle a\_{0}} ![{\displaystyle a_{0}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/693ad9f934775838bd72406b41ada4a59785d7ba) is zero.
-   The desired topology could also be derived from the following [metric](/wiki/Metric_space "Metric space"). The distance between distinct sequences ( a n ) , ( b n ) ∈ R N , {\\displaystyle (a\_{n}),(b\_{n})\\in R^{\\mathbb {N} },} ![{\displaystyle (a_{n}),(b_{n})\in R^{\mathbb {N} },}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/574ad13d985c0dcc8686ff4316fec9747b711f7f) is defined to be d ( ( a n ) , ( b n ) ) \= 2 − k , {\\displaystyle d((a\_{n}),(b\_{n}))=2^{-k},} ![{\displaystyle d((a_{n}),(b_{n}))=2^{-k},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6a8b4e845443184939549b7ffaefb60f916732a7) where k {\\displaystyle k} ![{\displaystyle k}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3c9a2c7b599b37105512c5d570edc034056dd40) is the smallest [natural number](/wiki/Natural_number "Natural number") such that a k ≠ b k {\\displaystyle a\_{k}\\neq b\_{k}} ![{\displaystyle a_{k}\neq b_{k}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b39546464207d480d4c74286e8c792cb150438be) ; the distance between two equal sequences is of course zero.

Informally, two sequences ( a n ) {\\displaystyle (a\_{n})} ![{\displaystyle (a_{n})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/18bc33c7c35d82b00f88d3a9103ed4738cde41f9) and ( b n ) {\\displaystyle (b\_{n})} ![{\displaystyle (b_{n})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5ca3872ee8054312f21ff267bc6831745e42b9c9) become closer and closer if and only if more and more of their terms agree exactly. Formally, the sequence of [partial sums](/wiki/Partial_sum "Partial sum") of some infinite summation converges if for every fixed power of X {\\displaystyle X} ![{\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab) the coefficient stabilizes: there is a point beyond which all further partial sums have the same coefficient. This is clearly the case for the right hand side of (**[1](#math_1)**), regardless of the values a n {\\displaystyle a\_{n}} ![{\displaystyle a_{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/790f9209748c2dca7ed7b81932c37c02af1dbc31) , since inclusion of the term for i \= n {\\displaystyle i=n} ![{\displaystyle i=n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0fd7cee5ae3994505fbd4c07a2cae9b2887e6d51) gives the last (and in fact only) change to the coefficient of X n {\\displaystyle X^{n}} ![{\displaystyle X^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/268db8293666fefd75cfb00513706171948edf09) . It is also obvious that the [limit](/wiki/Limit_of_a_sequence "Limit of a sequence") of the sequence of partial sums is equal to the left hand side.

This topological structure, together with the ring operations described above, form a topological ring. This is called the **ring of formal power series over R {\\displaystyle R} ![{\displaystyle R}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4b0bfb3769bf24d80e15374dc37b0441e2616e33) ** and is denoted by R \[ \[ X \] \] {\\displaystyle R\[\[X\]\]} ![{\displaystyle R[[X]]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fc00b68cd3f2c0346dea47d276cea5331fa55159) . The topology has the useful property that an infinite summation converges if and only if the sequence of its terms converges to 0, which just means that any fixed power of X {\\displaystyle X} ![{\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab) occurs in only finitely many terms.

The topological structure allows much more flexible usage of infinite summations. For instance the rule for multiplication can be restated simply as

( ∑ i ∈ N a i X i ) × ( ∑ i ∈ N b i X i ) \= ∑ i , j ∈ N a i b j X i + j , {\\displaystyle \\left(\\sum \_{i\\in \\mathbb {N} }a\_{i}X^{i}\\right)\\times \\left(\\sum \_{i\\in \\mathbb {N} }b\_{i}X^{i}\\right)=\\sum \_{i,j\\in \\mathbb {N} }a\_{i}b\_{j}X^{i+j},} ![{\displaystyle \left(\sum _{i\in \mathbb {N} }a_{i}X^{i}\right)\times \left(\sum _{i\in \mathbb {N} }b_{i}X^{i}\right)=\sum _{i,j\in \mathbb {N} }a_{i}b_{j}X^{i+j},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/46dfffae49cebf1ca53e78d148cab397c83929a4) 

since only finitely many terms on the right affect any fixed X n {\\displaystyle X^{n}} ![{\displaystyle X^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/268db8293666fefd75cfb00513706171948edf09) . Infinite products are also defined by the topological structure; it can be seen that an infinite product converges if and only if the sequence of its factors converges to 1 (in which case the product is nonzero) or infinitely many factors have no constant term (in which case the product is zero).

#### Alternative topologies

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Formal_power_series&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Alternative topologies")\]

The above topology is the [finest topology](/wiki/Comparison_of_topologies "Comparison of topologies") for which

∑ i \= 0 ∞ a i X i {\\displaystyle \\sum \_{i=0}^{\\infty }a\_{i}X^{i}} ![{\displaystyle \sum _{i=0}^{\infty }a_{i}X^{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d7e88354bfaac4143143da2fb567ca511e936d00) 

always converges as a summation to the formal power series designated by the same expression, and it often suffices to give a meaning to infinite sums and products, or other kinds of limits that one wishes to use to designate particular formal power series. It can however happen occasionally that one wishes to use a coarser topology, so that certain expressions become convergent that would otherwise diverge. This applies in particular when the base ring R {\\displaystyle R} ![{\displaystyle R}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4b0bfb3769bf24d80e15374dc37b0441e2616e33) already comes with a topology other than the discrete one, for instance if it is also a ring of formal power series.

In the ring of formal power series Z \[ \[ X \] \] \[ \[ Y \] \] {\\displaystyle \\mathbb {Z} \[\[X\]\]\[\[Y\]\]} ![{\displaystyle \mathbb {Z} [[X]][[Y]]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/46c700f48e94a967e746de9b5fd86509bf0866ce) , the topology of above construction only relates to the indeterminate Y {\\displaystyle Y} ![{\displaystyle Y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/961d67d6b454b4df2301ac571808a3538b3a6d3f) , since the topology that was put on Z \[ \[ X \] \] {\\displaystyle \\mathbb {Z} \[\[X\]\]} ![{\displaystyle \mathbb {Z} [[X]]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/db555c65d9ca63ab59cd9f4da79524993d56f08e) has been replaced by the discrete topology when defining the topology of the whole ring. So

∑ i \= 0 ∞ X Y i {\\displaystyle \\sum \_{i=0}^{\\infty }XY^{i}} ![{\displaystyle \sum _{i=0}^{\infty }XY^{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d6dbf6bf30f168a7b67e0006bef80501379eb2f8) 

converges (and its sum can be written as X 1 − Y {\\displaystyle {\\tfrac {X}{1-Y}}} ![{\displaystyle {\tfrac {X}{1-Y}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/579a9df8071940f91eff0f459629577afd33aa52) ); however

∑ i \= 0 ∞ X i Y {\\displaystyle \\sum \_{i=0}^{\\infty }X^{i}Y} ![{\displaystyle \sum _{i=0}^{\infty }X^{i}Y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1cb6df25748db0ed9a5afcc2e2f24d07ca0c4644) 

would be considered to be divergent, since every term affects the coefficient of Y {\\displaystyle Y} ![{\displaystyle Y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/961d67d6b454b4df2301ac571808a3538b3a6d3f) . This asymmetry disappears if the power series ring in Y {\\displaystyle Y} ![{\displaystyle Y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/961d67d6b454b4df2301ac571808a3538b3a6d3f) is given the product topology where each copy of Z \[ \[ X \] \] {\\displaystyle \\mathbb {Z} \[\[X\]\]} ![{\displaystyle \mathbb {Z} [[X]]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/db555c65d9ca63ab59cd9f4da79524993d56f08e) is given its topology as a ring of formal power series rather than the discrete topology. With this topology, a sequence of elements of Z \[ \[ X \] \] \[ \[ Y \] \] {\\displaystyle \\mathbb {Z} \[\[X\]\]\[\[Y\]\]} ![{\displaystyle \mathbb {Z} [[X]][[Y]]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/46c700f48e94a967e746de9b5fd86509bf0866ce) converges if the coefficient of each power of Y {\\displaystyle Y} ![{\displaystyle Y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/961d67d6b454b4df2301ac571808a3538b3a6d3f) converges to a formal power series in X {\\displaystyle X} ![{\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab) , a weaker condition than stabilizing entirely. For instance, with this topology, in the second example given above, the coefficient of Y {\\displaystyle Y} ![{\displaystyle Y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/961d67d6b454b4df2301ac571808a3538b3a6d3f) converges to 1 1 − X {\\displaystyle {\\tfrac {1}{1-X}}} ![{\displaystyle {\tfrac {1}{1-X}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fd7f8cd60de7cea555d018fa2f8807472b18a912) , so the whole summation converges to Y 1 − X {\\displaystyle {\\tfrac {Y}{1-X}}} ![{\displaystyle {\tfrac {Y}{1-X}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2c8c49cf37cf3c6a73a6b902ee320fe8976c8bfa) .

This way of defining the topology is in fact the standard one for repeated constructions of rings of formal power series, and gives the same topology as one would get by taking formal power series in all indeterminates at once. In the above example that would mean constructing Z \[ \[ X , Y \] \] {\\displaystyle \\mathbb {Z} \[\[X,Y\]\]} ![{\displaystyle \mathbb {Z} [[X,Y]]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d7a788024bf54b4a96e75da8a28dc68d90485b03) and here a sequence converges if and only if the coefficient of every monomial X i Y j {\\displaystyle X^{i}Y^{j}} ![{\displaystyle X^{i}Y^{j}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/374111bdb3546213b78c17cf90182be0b78bf083) stabilizes. This topology, which is also the I {\\displaystyle I} ![{\displaystyle I}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/535ea7fc4134a31cbe2251d9d3511374bc41be9f) \-adic topology, where I \= ( X , Y ) {\\displaystyle I=(X,Y)} ![{\displaystyle I=(X,Y)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fe1346658d6c1a7412b42bb03fa152243936bcf3) is the ideal generated by X {\\displaystyle X} ![{\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab) and Y {\\displaystyle Y} ![{\displaystyle Y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/961d67d6b454b4df2301ac571808a3538b3a6d3f) , still enjoys the property that a summation converges if and only if its terms tend to 0.

The same principle could be used to make other divergent limits converge. For instance in R \[ \[ X \] \] {\\displaystyle \\mathbb {R} \[\[X\]\]} ![{\displaystyle \mathbb {R} [[X]]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/28096a142fdd25bcadca1db012a77605c0e51274) the limit

lim n → ∞ ( 1 + X n ) n {\\displaystyle \\lim \_{n\\to \\infty }\\left(1+{\\frac {X}{n}}\\right)^{\\!n}} ![{\displaystyle \lim _{n\to \infty }\left(1+{\frac {X}{n}}\right)^{\!n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0010a02c8c1db6e375f20015712e197f5acbcd9d) 

does not exist, so in particular it does not converge to

exp ⁡ ( X ) \= ∑ n ∈ N X n n ! . {\\displaystyle \\exp(X)=\\sum \_{n\\in \\mathbb {N} }{\\frac {X^{n}}{n!}}.} ![{\displaystyle \exp(X)=\sum _{n\in \mathbb {N} }{\frac {X^{n}}{n!}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2160b59e2cb95f63b187e0762d4a5840db30ab62) 

This is because for i ≥ 2 {\\displaystyle i\\geq 2} ![{\displaystyle i\geq 2}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3aa8fcdf13fde4c390be2561ac45744d47a48cae) the coefficient ( n i ) / n i {\\displaystyle {\\tbinom {n}{i}}/n^{i}} ![{\displaystyle {\tbinom {n}{i}}/n^{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/93a6d2c6fc95a869f0f350632eed809c22f792a8) of X i {\\displaystyle X^{i}} ![{\displaystyle X^{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1ee700b37109a25ac72a3d5587c3b8a78b149c03) does not stabilize as n → ∞ {\\displaystyle n\\to \\infty } ![{\displaystyle n\to \infty }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a0d55d9b32f6fa8fab6a84ea444a6b5a24bb45e1) . It does however converge in the usual topology of R {\\displaystyle \\mathbb {R} } ![{\displaystyle \mathbb {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/786849c765da7a84dbc3cce43e96aad58a5868dc) , and in fact to the coefficient 1 i ! {\\displaystyle {\\tfrac {1}{i!}}} ![{\displaystyle {\tfrac {1}{i!}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5deaef2a7392027791cc8865a1ee02980a67a8a4) of exp ⁡ ( X ) {\\displaystyle \\exp(X)} ![{\displaystyle \exp(X)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/91cd111ee9dcf82fd9d42b526c7cccc5066bff12) . Therefore, if one would give R \[ \[ X \] \] {\\displaystyle \\mathbb {R} \[\[X\]\]} ![{\displaystyle \mathbb {R} [[X]]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/28096a142fdd25bcadca1db012a77605c0e51274) the product topology of R N {\\displaystyle \\mathbb {R} ^{\\mathbb {N} }} ![{\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{\mathbb {N} }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2608c429068c69675f1144f0ba8249603a8ceaf4) where the topology of R {\\displaystyle \\mathbb {R} } ![{\displaystyle \mathbb {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/786849c765da7a84dbc3cce43e96aad58a5868dc) is the usual topology rather than the discrete one, then the above limit would converge to exp ⁡ ( X ) {\\displaystyle \\exp(X)} ![{\displaystyle \exp(X)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/91cd111ee9dcf82fd9d42b526c7cccc5066bff12) . This more permissive approach is not however the standard when considering formal power series, as it would lead to convergence considerations that are as subtle as they are in [analysis](/wiki/Analysis_\(mathematics\) "Analysis (mathematics)"), while the philosophy of formal power series is on the contrary to make convergence questions as trivial as they can possibly be. With this topology it would _not_ be the case that a summation converges if and only if its terms tend to 0.

exp ⁡ ( X ) \= 1 + X + X 2 2 ! + X 3 3 ! + X 4 4 ! + ⋯ , {\\displaystyle \\exp(X)=1+X+{\\frac {X^{2}}{2!}}+{\\frac {X^{3}}{3!}}+{\\frac {X^{4}}{4!}}+\\cdots ,} ![{\displaystyle \exp(X)=1+X+{\frac {X^{2}}{2!}}+{\frac {X^{3}}{3!}}+{\frac {X^{4}}{4!}}+\cdots ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/32804c24f557c5b74130acb43dd9b97cb7a428b1) 

then the equality

exp ⁡ ( exp ⁡ ( X ) − 1 ) \= 1 + X + X 2 + 5 X 3 6 + 5 X 4 8 + ⋯ {\\displaystyle \\exp(\\exp(X)-1)=1+X+X^{2}+{\\frac {5X^{3}}{6}}+{\\frac {5X^{4}}{8}}+\\cdots } ![{\displaystyle \exp(\exp(X)-1)=1+X+X^{2}+{\frac {5X^{3}}{6}}+{\frac {5X^{4}}{8}}+\cdots }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/038fa1ad923ebe6cebcdf11f67874df5ea5b0bd8) 

makes perfect sense as a formal power series, since the constant coefficient of exp ⁡ ( X ) − 1 {\\displaystyle \\exp(X)-1} ![{\displaystyle \exp(X)-1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/10038336bd884da768dfecc330b109f1fa951b26) is zero.

### Composition inverse

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Formal_power_series&action=edit&section=15 "Edit section: Composition inverse")\]

Whenever a formal series

f ( X ) \= ∑ k f k X k ∈ R \[ \[ X \] \] {\\displaystyle f(X)=\\sum \_{k}f\_{k}X^{k}\\in R\[\[X\]\]} ![{\displaystyle f(X)=\sum _{k}f_{k}X^{k}\in R[[X]]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/498d0e5195848351dcd28c4fbcda6d532cac1c7d) 

has _f_0 = 0 and _f_1 being an invertible element of _R_, there exists a series

g ( X ) \= ∑ k g k X k {\\displaystyle g(X)=\\sum \_{k}g\_{k}X^{k}} ![{\displaystyle g(X)=\sum _{k}g_{k}X^{k}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d48f7d4e19e6c77ee52eb77caefb617033610f20) 

that is the [composition inverse](/wiki/Composition_inverse "Composition inverse") of f {\\displaystyle f} ![{\displaystyle f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61) , meaning that composing f {\\displaystyle f} ![{\displaystyle f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61) with g {\\displaystyle g} ![{\displaystyle g}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d3556280e66fe2c0d0140df20935a6f057381d77) gives the series representing the [identity function](/wiki/Identity_function "Identity function") x \= 0 + 1 x + 0 x 2 + 0 x 3 + ⋯ {\\displaystyle x=0+1x+0x^{2}+0x^{3}+\\cdots } ![{\displaystyle x=0+1x+0x^{2}+0x^{3}+\cdots }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6372f48a22e0c472a795d102a31e9738bbf28296) . The coefficients of g {\\displaystyle g} ![{\displaystyle g}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d3556280e66fe2c0d0140df20935a6f057381d77) may be found recursively by using the above formula for the coefficients of a composition, equating them with those of the composition identity _X_ (that is 1 at degree 1 and 0 at every degree greater than 1). In the case when the coefficient ring is a field of characteristic 0, the [Lagrange inversion formula](#The_Lagrange_inversion_formula) (discussed below) provides a powerful tool to compute the coefficients of _g_, as well as the coefficients of the (multiplicative) powers of _g_.

### Formal differentiation

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Formal_power_series&action=edit&section=16 "Edit section: Formal differentiation")\]

Given a formal power series

f \= ∑ n ≥ 0 a n X n ∈ R \[ \[ X \] \] , {\\displaystyle f=\\sum \_{n\\geq 0}a\_{n}X^{n}\\in R\[\[X\]\],} ![{\displaystyle f=\sum _{n\geq 0}a_{n}X^{n}\in R[[X]],}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/da8f977d0e0948457a651e68daaf51628e4cdf21) 

we define its **[formal derivative](/wiki/Formal_derivative "Formal derivative")**, denoted _Df_ or _f_ ′, by

D f \= f ′ \= ∑ n ≥ 1 a n n X n − 1 . {\\displaystyle Df=f'=\\sum \_{n\\geq 1}a\_{n}nX^{n-1}.} ![{\displaystyle Df=f'=\sum _{n\geq 1}a_{n}nX^{n-1}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0a351084703507c68f7b1aaaa9d7d84217f28559) 

The symbol _D_ is called the **formal differentiation operator**. This definition simply mimics term-by-term differentiation of a polynomial.

This operation is _R_\-[linear](/wiki/Linear_operator "Linear operator"):

D ( a f + b g ) \= a ⋅ D f + b ⋅ D g {\\displaystyle D(af+bg)=a\\cdot Df+b\\cdot Dg} ![{\displaystyle D(af+bg)=a\cdot Df+b\cdot Dg}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/720ca21359e8c3828db44171c26611b4fb88246d) 

for any _a_, _b_ in _R_ and any _f_, _g_ in R \[ \[ X \] \] . {\\displaystyle R\[\[X\]\].} ![{\displaystyle R[[X]].}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c8e6613b10be85c384bce093c9509bd654ee3448) Additionally, the formal derivative has many of the properties of the usual [derivative](/wiki/Derivative "Derivative") of calculus. For example, the [product rule](/wiki/Product_rule "Product rule") is valid:

D ( f g )   \=   f ⋅ ( D g ) + ( D f ) ⋅ g , {\\displaystyle D(fg)\\ =\\ f\\cdot (Dg)+(Df)\\cdot g,} ![{\displaystyle D(fg)\ =\ f\cdot (Dg)+(Df)\cdot g,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/02d0be5342899375c90029bfe7067dbfe3f698c6) 

and the [chain rule](/wiki/Chain_rule "Chain rule") works as well:

D ( f ∘ g ) \= ( D f ∘ g ) ⋅ D g , {\\displaystyle D(f\\circ g)=(Df\\circ g)\\cdot Dg,} ![{\displaystyle D(f\circ g)=(Df\circ g)\cdot Dg,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e1a5d49c4ab89b598652ab9b3851a8b4a2b3c0b7) 

whenever the appropriate compositions of series are defined (see above under [composition of series](#Composition_of_series)).

Thus, in these respects formal power series behave like [Taylor series](/wiki/Taylor_series "Taylor series"). Indeed, for the _f_ defined above, we find that

( D k f ) ( 0 ) \= k ! a k , {\\displaystyle (D^{k}f)(0)=k!a\_{k},} ![{\displaystyle (D^{k}f)(0)=k!a_{k},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/380a7bfef816dfc532bec6c0f8f291bbcbe1906e) 

where _D__k_ denotes the _k_th formal derivative (that is, the result of formally differentiating _k_ times).

### Formal antidifferentiation

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Formal_power_series&action=edit&section=17 "Edit section: Formal antidifferentiation")\]

If R {\\displaystyle R} ![{\displaystyle R}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4b0bfb3769bf24d80e15374dc37b0441e2616e33) is a ring with characteristic zero and the nonzero integers are invertible in R {\\displaystyle R} ![{\displaystyle R}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4b0bfb3769bf24d80e15374dc37b0441e2616e33) , then given a formal power series

f \= ∑ n ≥ 0 a n X n ∈ R \[ \[ X \] \] , {\\displaystyle f=\\sum \_{n\\geq 0}a\_{n}X^{n}\\in R\[\[X\]\],} ![{\displaystyle f=\sum _{n\geq 0}a_{n}X^{n}\in R[[X]],}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/da8f977d0e0948457a651e68daaf51628e4cdf21) 

we define its **formal antiderivative** or **formal indefinite integral** by

D − 1 f \= ∫ f   d X \= C + ∑ n ≥ 0 a n X n + 1 n + 1 . {\\displaystyle D^{-1}f=\\int f\\ dX=C+\\sum \_{n\\geq 0}a\_{n}{\\frac {X^{n+1}}{n+1}}.} ![{\displaystyle D^{-1}f=\int f\ dX=C+\sum _{n\geq 0}a_{n}{\frac {X^{n+1}}{n+1}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3aacb5e8e47b35f5a78914e99d1b4ecbd6cc9858) 

for any constant C ∈ R {\\displaystyle C\\in R} ![{\displaystyle C\in R}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9fb13789dc84128f9573d24564d97850ac982b74) .

This operation is _R_\-[linear](/wiki/Linear_operator "Linear operator"):

D − 1 ( a f + b g ) \= a ⋅ D − 1 f + b ⋅ D − 1 g {\\displaystyle D^{-1}(af+bg)=a\\cdot D^{-1}f+b\\cdot D^{-1}g} ![{\displaystyle D^{-1}(af+bg)=a\cdot D^{-1}f+b\cdot D^{-1}g}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/166edf81826f3ec42212eed20b8447ebcbc43b33) 

for any _a_, _b_ in _R_ and any _f_, _g_ in R \[ \[ X \] \] . {\\displaystyle R\[\[X\]\].} ![{\displaystyle R[[X]].}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c8e6613b10be85c384bce093c9509bd654ee3448) Additionally, the formal antiderivative has many of the properties of the usual [antiderivative](/wiki/Antiderivative "Antiderivative") of calculus. For example, the formal antiderivative is the [right inverse](/wiki/Inverse_function#Left_and_right_inverses "Inverse function") of the formal derivative:

D ( D − 1 ( f ) ) \= f {\\displaystyle D(D^{-1}(f))=f} ![{\displaystyle D(D^{-1}(f))=f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/77286f8e3b0163b0acc29bae4a073387768fc9ac) 

for any f ∈ R \[ \[ X \] \] {\\displaystyle f\\in R\[\[X\]\]} ![{\displaystyle f\in R[[X]]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2058668a3b1aee4e7cb7df2913c300134ffb2056) .

### Topological properties of the formal power series ring

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Formal_power_series&action=edit&section=20 "Edit section: Topological properties of the formal power series ring")\]

The metric space ( R \[ \[ X \] \] , d ) {\\displaystyle (R\[\[X\]\],d)} ![{\displaystyle (R[[X]],d)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b7f3075cede0445026952397334388a0494a524b) is [complete](/wiki/Completeness_\(topology\) "Completeness (topology)").

The ring R \[ \[ X \] \] {\\displaystyle R\[\[X\]\]} ![{\displaystyle R[[X]]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fc00b68cd3f2c0346dea47d276cea5331fa55159) is [compact](/wiki/Compact_space "Compact space") if and only if _R_ is [finite](/wiki/Finite_set "Finite set"). This follows from [Tychonoff's theorem](/wiki/Tychonoff%27s_theorem "Tychonoff's theorem") and the characterisation of the topology on R \[ \[ X \] \] {\\displaystyle R\[\[X\]\]} ![{\displaystyle R[[X]]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fc00b68cd3f2c0346dea47d276cea5331fa55159) as a product topology.

### Weierstrass preparation

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Formal_power_series&action=edit&section=21 "Edit section: Weierstrass preparation")\]

Main article: [Weierstrass preparation theorem § Formal power series in complete local rings](/wiki/Weierstrass_preparation_theorem#Formal_power_series_in_complete_local_rings "Weierstrass preparation theorem")

The ring of formal power series with coefficients in a [complete local ring](/wiki/Complete_local_ring "Complete local ring") satisfies the [Weierstrass preparation theorem](/wiki/Weierstrass_preparation_theorem "Weierstrass preparation theorem").

## Applications

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Formal_power_series&action=edit&section=22 "Edit section: Applications")\]

Formal power series can be used to solve recurrences occurring in [number theory](/wiki/Number_theory "Number theory") and combinatorics. For an example involving finding a closed form expression for the [Fibonacci numbers](/wiki/Fibonacci_number "Fibonacci number"), see the article on [Examples of generating functions](/wiki/Examples_of_generating_functions "Examples of generating functions").

In [mathematical analysis](/wiki/Mathematical_analysis "Mathematical analysis"), every convergent [power series](/wiki/Power_series "Power series") defines a [function](/wiki/Function_\(mathematics\) "Function (mathematics)") with values in the [real](/wiki/Real_number "Real number") or [complex](/wiki/Complex_number "Complex number") numbers. Formal power series over certain special rings can also be interpreted as functions, but one has to be careful with the [domain](/wiki/Function_domain "Function domain") and [codomain](/wiki/Codomain "Codomain"). Let

f \= ∑ a n X n ∈ R \[ \[ X \] \] , {\\displaystyle f=\\sum a\_{n}X^{n}\\in R\[\[X\]\],} ![{\displaystyle f=\sum a_{n}X^{n}\in R[[X]],}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/303895e99a9af301b1a2827269a6e77e26c4b0bf) 

and suppose S {\\displaystyle S} ![{\displaystyle S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4611d85173cd3b508e67077d4a1252c9c05abca2) is a commutative associative algebra over R {\\displaystyle R} ![{\displaystyle R}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4b0bfb3769bf24d80e15374dc37b0441e2616e33) , I {\\displaystyle I} ![{\displaystyle I}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/535ea7fc4134a31cbe2251d9d3511374bc41be9f) is an ideal in S {\\displaystyle S} ![{\displaystyle S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4611d85173cd3b508e67077d4a1252c9c05abca2) such that the [I-adic topology](/wiki/I-adic_topology "I-adic topology") on S {\\displaystyle S} ![{\displaystyle S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4611d85173cd3b508e67077d4a1252c9c05abca2) is complete, and x {\\displaystyle x} ![{\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4) is an element of I {\\displaystyle I} ![{\displaystyle I}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/535ea7fc4134a31cbe2251d9d3511374bc41be9f) . Define:

f ( x ) \= ∑ n ≥ 0 a n x n . {\\displaystyle f(x)=\\sum \_{n\\geq 0}a\_{n}x^{n}.} ![{\displaystyle f(x)=\sum _{n\geq 0}a_{n}x^{n}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/15a5c0c725bb1cd4f94eda69b0aee2e682aa46b6) 

This series is guaranteed to converge in S {\\displaystyle S} ![{\displaystyle S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4611d85173cd3b508e67077d4a1252c9c05abca2) given the above assumptions on x {\\displaystyle x} ![{\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4) . Furthermore, we have

( f + g ) ( x ) \= f ( x ) + g ( x ) {\\displaystyle (f+g)(x)=f(x)+g(x)} ![{\displaystyle (f+g)(x)=f(x)+g(x)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cf80cb50218eac1e40d4a0908bd039db3bd0863c) 

and

( f g ) ( x ) \= f ( x ) g ( x ) . {\\displaystyle (fg)(x)=f(x)g(x).} ![{\displaystyle (fg)(x)=f(x)g(x).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4006271010fa8b94c9f70e16055e32e0520cd18c) 

Unlike in the case of bona fide functions, these formulas are not definitions but have to be proved.

Since the topology on R \[ \[ X \] \] {\\displaystyle R\[\[X\]\]} ![{\displaystyle R[[X]]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fc00b68cd3f2c0346dea47d276cea5331fa55159) is the ( X ) {\\displaystyle (X)} ![{\displaystyle (X)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/42d39792a8f026fb4cffe772ca5ba3dde00930f7) \-adic topology and R \[ \[ X \] \] {\\displaystyle R\[\[X\]\]} ![{\displaystyle R[[X]]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fc00b68cd3f2c0346dea47d276cea5331fa55159) is complete, we can in particular apply power series to other power series, provided that the arguments don't have [constant coefficients](/wiki/Constant_coefficients "Constant coefficients") (so that they belong to the ideal ( X ) {\\displaystyle (X)} ![{\displaystyle (X)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/42d39792a8f026fb4cffe772ca5ba3dde00930f7) ): f ( 0 ) {\\displaystyle f(0)} ![{\displaystyle f(0)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bc54adf96acf7e2466de00b7739144d36840108f) , f ( X 2 − X ) {\\displaystyle f(X^{2}-X)} ![{\displaystyle f(X^{2}-X)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ff758187919c9e756f3ad1c3fec9f96de5eb1d84) and f ( ( 1 − X ) − 1 − 1 ) {\\displaystyle f((1-X)^{-1}-1)} ![{\displaystyle f((1-X)^{-1}-1)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/da2e6ac4eb3fba5106e2b902f99c0d9530ba79cb) are all well defined for any formal power series f ∈ R \[ \[ X \] \] . {\\displaystyle f\\in R\[\[X\]\].} ![{\displaystyle f\in R[[X]].}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0203577af2046e95b374f326982d01a5c0747352) 

With this formalism, we can give an explicit formula for the multiplicative inverse of a power series f {\\displaystyle f} ![{\displaystyle f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61) whose constant coefficient a \= f ( 0 ) {\\displaystyle a=f(0)} ![{\displaystyle a=f(0)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/daca34adc087d340bcdac358be459bc3a4b10bf4) is invertible in R {\\displaystyle R} ![{\displaystyle R}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4b0bfb3769bf24d80e15374dc37b0441e2616e33) :

f − 1 \= ∑ n ≥ 0 a − n − 1 ( a − f ) n . {\\displaystyle f^{-1}=\\sum \_{n\\geq 0}a^{-n-1}(a-f)^{n}.} ![{\displaystyle f^{-1}=\sum _{n\geq 0}a^{-n-1}(a-f)^{n}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0d936676e0bd26c064010eb3b49a771732786b9a) 

If the formal power series g {\\displaystyle g} ![{\displaystyle g}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d3556280e66fe2c0d0140df20935a6f057381d77) with g ( 0 ) \= 0 {\\displaystyle g(0)=0} ![{\displaystyle g(0)=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/679b33e306b0721551440784cbc6b73cc13c77d1) is given implicitly by the equation

f ( g ) \= X {\\displaystyle f(g)=X} ![{\displaystyle f(g)=X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/99d5484ab623720a76318200d6c79e67817e2122) 

where f {\\displaystyle f} ![{\displaystyle f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61) is a known power series with f ( 0 ) \= 0 {\\displaystyle f(0)=0} ![{\displaystyle f(0)=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8d308c32c9894b88115262081194321ae7d9bbf3) , then the coefficients of g {\\displaystyle g} ![{\displaystyle g}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d3556280e66fe2c0d0140df20935a6f057381d77) can be explicitly computed using the [Lagrange inversion formula](#The_Lagrange_inversion_formula).

### Formal Laurent series

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Formal_power_series&action=edit&section=25 "Edit section: Formal Laurent series")\]

The **formal Laurent series** over a ring R {\\displaystyle R} ![{\displaystyle R}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4b0bfb3769bf24d80e15374dc37b0441e2616e33) are defined in a similar way to a formal power series, except that we also allow finitely many terms of negative degree. That is, they are the series that can be written as

f \= ∑ n \= N ∞ a n X n {\\displaystyle f=\\sum \_{n=N}^{\\infty }a\_{n}X^{n}} ![{\displaystyle f=\sum _{n=N}^{\infty }a_{n}X^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/541e505fec43345fec69f7feac24e9061cb87213) 

for some integer N {\\displaystyle N} ![{\displaystyle N}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f5e3890c981ae85503089652feb48b191b57aae3) , so that there are only finitely many negative n {\\displaystyle n} ![{\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b) with a n ≠ 0 {\\displaystyle a\_{n}\\neq 0} ![{\displaystyle a_{n}\neq 0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5cf50204372c4d2e24c37aace0035de48b877973) . (This is different from the classical [Laurent series](/wiki/Laurent_series "Laurent series") of [complex analysis](/wiki/Complex_analysis "Complex analysis").) For a non-zero formal Laurent series, the minimal integer n {\\displaystyle n} ![{\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b) such that a n ≠ 0 {\\displaystyle a\_{n}\\neq 0} ![{\displaystyle a_{n}\neq 0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5cf50204372c4d2e24c37aace0035de48b877973) is called the _order_ of f {\\displaystyle f} ![{\displaystyle f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61) and is denoted ord ⁡ ( f ) . {\\displaystyle \\operatorname {ord} (f).} ![{\displaystyle \operatorname {ord} (f).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a92c91baced97aabd5993d5b60f5129c647ee23c) (The order ord(0) of the zero series is + ∞ {\\displaystyle +\\infty } ![{\displaystyle +\infty }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bddbb0e4420a7e744cf71bd71216e11b0bf88831) .)

For instance, X − 3 + 1 2 X − 2 + 1 3 X − 1 + 1 4 + 1 5 X + 1 6 X 2 + 1 7 X 3 + 1 8 X 4 + … {\\displaystyle X^{-3}+{\\frac {1}{2}}X^{-2}+{\\frac {1}{3}}X^{-1}+{\\frac {1}{4}}+{\\frac {1}{5}}X+{\\frac {1}{6}}X^{2}+{\\frac {1}{7}}X^{3}+{\\frac {1}{8}}X^{4}+\\dots } ![{\displaystyle X^{-3}+{\frac {1}{2}}X^{-2}+{\frac {1}{3}}X^{-1}+{\frac {1}{4}}+{\frac {1}{5}}X+{\frac {1}{6}}X^{2}+{\frac {1}{7}}X^{3}+{\frac {1}{8}}X^{4}+\dots }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/18fbf17a6bfe5c890ca5e1ef48d923862c3d16f0) is a formal Laurent series of order –3.

Multiplication of such series can be defined. Indeed, similarly to the definition for formal power series, the coefficient of X k {\\displaystyle X^{k}} ![{\displaystyle X^{k}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/26829a7c0c84cd8a07f32ae14897a39dffb8f577) of two series with respective sequences of coefficients { a n } {\\displaystyle \\{a\_{n}\\}} ![{\displaystyle \{a_{n}\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/339d461b497fb8f8670bb29308fe09f0e7bfd34a) and { b n } {\\displaystyle \\{b\_{n}\\}} ![{\displaystyle \{b_{n}\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cad2485b9672375982ec521a53ee5a4104001a15) is ∑ i ∈ Z a i b k − i . {\\displaystyle \\sum \_{i\\in \\mathbb {Z} }a\_{i}b\_{k-i}.} ![{\displaystyle \sum _{i\in \mathbb {Z} }a_{i}b_{k-i}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/90253ff0a6ce463fa2d7127bfa8d88a3ad6cb3b1) This sum has only finitely many nonzero terms because of the assumed vanishing of coefficients at sufficiently negative indices.

The formal Laurent series form the **ring of formal Laurent series** over R {\\displaystyle R} ![{\displaystyle R}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4b0bfb3769bf24d80e15374dc37b0441e2616e33) , denoted by R ( ( X ) ) {\\displaystyle R((X))} ![{\displaystyle R((X))}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ca55ccda99acec9e5c4a2e2d6fd5d9378b7ae11e) .[\[b\]](#cite_note-7) It is equal to the [localization](/wiki/Localization_of_a_ring "Localization of a ring") of the ring R \[ \[ X \] \] {\\displaystyle R\[\[X\]\]} ![{\displaystyle R[[X]]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fc00b68cd3f2c0346dea47d276cea5331fa55159) of formal power series with respect to the set of positive powers of X {\\displaystyle X} ![{\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab) . If R \= K {\\displaystyle R=K} ![{\displaystyle R=K}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3a4b15b00c9af207941ea7caba435beaee819045) is a [field](/wiki/Field_\(mathematics\) "Field (mathematics)"), then K ( ( X ) ) {\\displaystyle K((X))} ![{\displaystyle K((X))}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b0d7da50a1e8addf8dc2318572b36468488665dd) is in fact a field, which may alternatively be obtained as the [field of fractions](/wiki/Field_of_fractions "Field of fractions") of the [integral domain](/wiki/Integral_domain "Integral domain") K \[ \[ X \] \] {\\displaystyle K\[\[X\]\]} ![{\displaystyle K[[X]]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/829385e112c1f43b691f221a8c47437b3ec65e68) .

As with R \[ \[ X \] \] {\\displaystyle R\[\[X\]\]} ![{\displaystyle R[[X]]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fc00b68cd3f2c0346dea47d276cea5331fa55159) , the ring R ( ( X ) ) {\\displaystyle R((X))} ![{\displaystyle R((X))}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ca55ccda99acec9e5c4a2e2d6fd5d9378b7ae11e) of formal Laurent series may be endowed with the structure of a topological ring by introducing the metric d ( f , g ) \= 2 − ord ⁡ ( f − g ) . {\\displaystyle d(f,g)=2^{-\\operatorname {ord} (f-g)}.} ![{\displaystyle d(f,g)=2^{-\operatorname {ord} (f-g)}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d5b25685fbebf05c35fe093aae99450c89b3fec8) (In particular, ord ⁡ ( 0 ) \= + ∞ {\\displaystyle \\operatorname {ord} (0)=+\\infty } ![{\displaystyle \operatorname {ord} (0)=+\infty }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4479134c3581380b713346041f0c52041690fad6) implies that d ( f , f ) \= 2 − ord ⁡ ( 0 ) \= 0 {\\displaystyle d(f,f)=2^{-\\operatorname {ord} (0)}=0} ![{\displaystyle d(f,f)=2^{-\operatorname {ord} (0)}=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8ce3e3e6582f6fbdb60892f9dc94d8735433c58c) .)

One may define formal differentiation for formal Laurent series in the natural (term-by-term) way. Precisely, the formal derivative of the formal Laurent series f {\\displaystyle f} ![{\displaystyle f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61) above is f ′ \= D f \= ∑ n ∈ Z n a n X n − 1 , {\\displaystyle f'=Df=\\sum \_{n\\in \\mathbb {Z} }na\_{n}X^{n-1},} ![{\displaystyle f'=Df=\sum _{n\in \mathbb {Z} }na_{n}X^{n-1},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/41fd7d9839ad546c617f55e2d63d81f0d6d71f90) which is again a formal Laurent series. If f {\\displaystyle f} ![{\displaystyle f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61) is a non-constant formal Laurent series and with coefficients in a field of characteristic 0, then one has ord ⁡ ( f ′ ) \= ord ⁡ ( f ) − 1. {\\displaystyle \\operatorname {ord} (f')=\\operatorname {ord} (f)-1.} ![{\displaystyle \operatorname {ord} (f')=\operatorname {ord} (f)-1.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/def6a255d6db0ac6c152c1df65f0bfda0fab819e) However, in general this is not the case since the factor n {\\displaystyle n} ![{\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b) for the lowest order term could be equal to 0 in R {\\displaystyle R} ![{\displaystyle R}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4b0bfb3769bf24d80e15374dc37b0441e2616e33) .

### The Lagrange inversion formula

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Formal_power_series&action=edit&section=27 "Edit section: The Lagrange inversion formula")\]

Main article: [Lagrange inversion theorem](/wiki/Lagrange_inversion_theorem "Lagrange inversion theorem")

As mentioned above, any formal series f ∈ K \[ \[ X \] \] {\\displaystyle f\\in K\[\[X\]\]} ![{\displaystyle f\in K[[X]]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/926bb96837aadb95685666bd8dc05fddf44590cb) with _f_0 = 0 and _f_1 ≠ 0 has a composition inverse g ∈ K \[ \[ X \] \] . {\\displaystyle g\\in K\[\[X\]\].} ![{\displaystyle g\in K[[X]].}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/38b2a9a83fdcb216c43a6a9bdbc5801c40a30c58) The following relation between the coefficients of _gn_ and _f_−_k_ holds ("Lagrange inversion formula"):

k \[ X k \] g n \= n \[ X − n \] f − k . {\\displaystyle k\[X^{k}\]g^{n}=n\[X^{-n}\]f^{-k}.} ![{\displaystyle k[X^{k}]g^{n}=n[X^{-n}]f^{-k}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f0b09b4a64523af8d15b44686d53d0c4b68b684d) 

In particular, for _n_ = 1 and all _k_ ≥ 1,

\[ X k \] g \= 1 k Res ⁡ ( f − k ) . {\\displaystyle \[X^{k}\]g={\\frac {1}{k}}\\operatorname {Res} \\left(f^{-k}\\right).} ![{\displaystyle [X^{k}]g={\frac {1}{k}}\operatorname {Res} \left(f^{-k}\right).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6fcef65a0aa284e766304c10a3bcd095dd935950)

#### Topology

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Formal_power_series&action=edit&section=29 "Edit section: Topology")\]

The topology on R \[ \[ X I \] \] {\\displaystyle R\[\[X\_{I}\]\]} ![{\displaystyle R[[X_{I}]]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0212328e3baa12585f53c4ad48cb4e392ec5ba19) is such that a sequence of its elements converges only if for each monomial _X_α the corresponding coefficient stabilizes. If _I_ is finite, then this the _J_\-adic topology, where _J_ is the ideal of R \[ \[ X I \] \] {\\displaystyle R\[\[X\_{I}\]\]} ![{\displaystyle R[[X_{I}]]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0212328e3baa12585f53c4ad48cb4e392ec5ba19) generated by all the indeterminates in _XI_. This does not hold if _I_ is infinite. For example, if I \= N , {\\displaystyle I=\\mathbb {N} ,} ![{\displaystyle I=\mathbb {N} ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f9009d7a643650eb73993d2557f06a8c1ccf3279) then the sequence ( f n ) n ∈ N {\\displaystyle (f\_{n})\_{n\\in \\mathbb {N} }} ![{\displaystyle (f_{n})_{n\in \mathbb {N} }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d822f0926e32e090d67100eca20476c144bee03a) with f n \= X n + X n + 1 + X n + 2 + ⋯ {\\displaystyle f\_{n}=X\_{n}+X\_{n+1}+X\_{n+2}+\\cdots } ![{\displaystyle f_{n}=X_{n}+X_{n+1}+X_{n+2}+\cdots }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/18184bb365e33b015ef5bc94ef7337091471a7e5) does not converge with respect to any _J_\-adic topology on _R_, but clearly for each monomial the corresponding coefficient stabilizes.

As remarked above, the topology on a repeated formal power series ring like R \[ \[ X \] \] \[ \[ Y \] \] {\\displaystyle R\[\[X\]\]\[\[Y\]\]} ![{\displaystyle R[[X]][[Y]]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1e78afafaa31beeca796142ebb8b545a17fe6e34) is usually chosen in such a way that it becomes isomorphic as a [topological ring](/wiki/Topological_ring "Topological ring") to R \[ \[ X , Y \] \] . {\\displaystyle R\[\[X,Y\]\].} ![{\displaystyle R[[X,Y]].}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/943a6d7c369f22b997619bd597351f7529a90eb7) 

#### Operations

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Formal_power_series&action=edit&section=30 "Edit section: Operations")\]

All of the operations defined for series in one variable may be extended to the several variables case.

-   A series is invertible if and only if its constant term is invertible in _R_.
-   The composition _f_(_g_(_X_)) of two series _f_ and _g_ is defined if _f_ is a series in a single indeterminate, and the constant term of _g_ is zero. For a series _f_ in several indeterminates a form of "composition" can similarly be defined, with as many separate series in the place of _g_ as there are indeterminates.

In the case of the formal derivative, there are now separate [partial derivative](/wiki/Partial_derivative "Partial derivative") operators, which differentiate with respect to each of the indeterminates. They all commute with each other.

#### Universal property

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Formal_power_series&action=edit&section=31 "Edit section: Universal property")\]

In the several variables case, the universal property characterizing R \[ \[ X 1 , … , X r \] \] {\\displaystyle R\[\[X\_{1},\\ldots ,X\_{r}\]\]} ![{\displaystyle R[[X_{1},\ldots ,X_{r}]]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5088b6c62e4e9b8b6ded87921dc418154335fa12) becomes the following. If _S_ is a commutative associative algebra over _R_, if _I_ is an ideal of _S_ such that the _I_\-adic topology on _S_ is complete, and if _x_1, ..., _xr_ are elements of _I_, then there is a _unique_ map Φ : R \[ \[ X 1 , … , X r \] \] → S {\\displaystyle \\Phi :R\[\[X\_{1},\\ldots ,X\_{r}\]\]\\to S} ![{\displaystyle \Phi :R[[X_{1},\ldots ,X_{r}]]\to S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/793bebc5253333e0fb24bb01cebf95a1cd96c12e) with the following properties:

-   Φ is an _R_\-algebra homomorphism
-   Φ is continuous
-   Φ(_X__i_) = _x__i_ for _i_ = 1, ..., _r_.

### Non-commuting variables

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Formal_power_series&action=edit&section=32 "Edit section: Non-commuting variables")\]

The several variable case can be further generalised by taking _non-commuting variables_ _Xi_ for _i_ ∈ _I_, where _I_ is an index set and then a [monomial](/wiki/Monomial "Monomial") _X_α is any [word](/wiki/Word_\(mathematics\) "Word (mathematics)") in the _XI_; a formal power series in _XI_ with coefficients in a ring _R_ is determined by any mapping from the set of monomials _X_α to a corresponding coefficient _c_α, and is denoted ∑ α c α X α {\\displaystyle \\textstyle \\sum \_{\\alpha }c\_{\\alpha }X^{\\alpha }} ![{\displaystyle \textstyle \sum _{\alpha }c_{\alpha }X^{\alpha }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5b9411567f5f2afb440f5171a11b19868cd94aa5) . ( ∑ α c α X α ) + ( ∑ α d α X α ) \= ∑ α ( c α + d α ) X α {\\displaystyle \\left(\\sum \_{\\alpha }c\_{\\alpha }X^{\\alpha }\\right)+\\left(\\sum \_{\\alpha }d\_{\\alpha }X^{\\alpha }\\right)=\\sum \_{\\alpha }(c\_{\\alpha }+d\_{\\alpha })X^{\\alpha }} ![{\displaystyle \left(\sum _{\alpha }c_{\alpha }X^{\alpha }\right)+\left(\sum _{\alpha }d_{\alpha }X^{\alpha }\right)=\sum _{\alpha }(c_{\alpha }+d_{\alpha })X^{\alpha }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/00e79832b72fd90b916aaa56174ea061582c9e16)

If R {\\displaystyle R} ![{\displaystyle R}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4b0bfb3769bf24d80e15374dc37b0441e2616e33) is a field, then so is R ( ( G ) ) {\\displaystyle R((G))} ![{\displaystyle R((G))}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b2b468a07b76b6e0695055bc686d723ed7c93900) . If R {\\displaystyle R} ![{\displaystyle R}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4b0bfb3769bf24d80e15374dc37b0441e2616e33) is an [ordered field](/wiki/Ordered_field "Ordered field"), we can order R ( ( G ) ) {\\displaystyle R((G))} ![{\displaystyle R((G))}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b2b468a07b76b6e0695055bc686d723ed7c93900) by setting any element to have the same sign as its leading coefficient, defined as the least element of the index set **I** associated to a non-zero coefficient. Finally if G {\\displaystyle G} ![{\displaystyle G}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f5f3c8921a3b352de45446a6789b104458c9f90b) is a [divisible group](/wiki/Divisible_group "Divisible group") and R {\\displaystyle R} ![{\displaystyle R}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4b0bfb3769bf24d80e15374dc37b0441e2616e33) is a [real closed field](/wiki/Real_closed_field "Real closed field"), then R ( ( G ) ) {\\displaystyle R((G))} ![{\displaystyle R((G))}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b2b468a07b76b6e0695055bc686d723ed7c93900) is a real closed field, and if R {\\displaystyle R} ![{\displaystyle R}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4b0bfb3769bf24d80e15374dc37b0441e2616e33) is [algebraically closed](/wiki/Algebraically_closed "Algebraically closed"), then so is R ( ( G ) ) {\\displaystyle R((G))} ![{\displaystyle R((G))}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b2b468a07b76b6e0695055bc686d723ed7c93900) .

This theory is due to [Hans Hahn](/wiki/Hans_Hahn_\(mathematician\) "Hans Hahn (mathematician)"), who also showed that one obtains subfields when the number of (non-zero) terms is bounded by some fixed infinite cardinality.

## Examples and related topics

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Formal_power_series&action=edit&section=35 "Edit section: Examples and related topics")\]

-   [Bell series](/wiki/Bell_series "Bell series") are used to study the properties of [multiplicative arithmetic functions](/wiki/Multiplicative_function "Multiplicative function")
-   [Formal groups](/wiki/Formal_group "Formal group") are used to define an abstract group law using formal power series
-   [Puiseux series](/wiki/Puiseux_series "Puiseux series") are an extension of formal Laurent series, allowing fractional exponents

## Notes

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Formal_power_series&action=edit&section=37 "Edit section: Notes")\]

1.  **[^](#cite_ref-6 "Jump up")** The formula is often attributed to [J.C.P. Miller](/wiki/J.C.P._Miller "J.C.P. Miller"), but it has a long history of rediscovery, dating back to least Euler's discovery in 1748.[\[4\]](#cite_note-Gould-4)
2.  **[^](#cite_ref-7 "Jump up")** For each nonzero formal Laurent series, the order is an integer (that is, the degrees of the terms are bounded below). But the ring R ( ( X ) ) {\\displaystyle R((X))} ![{\displaystyle R((X))}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ca55ccda99acec9e5c4a2e2d6fd5d9378b7ae11e) contains series of all orders.
3.  **[^](#cite_ref-11 "Jump up")** A number of different proofs exist, using techniques including Cauchy's coefficient formula for holomorphic functions, tree-counting arguments, or induction.[\[6\]](#cite_note-8)\[_[pages needed](/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources "Wikipedia:Citing sources")_\][\[7\]](#cite_note-9)[\[8\]](#cite_note-10)
