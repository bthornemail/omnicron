The 369 free octominoes

An **octomino** (or **8-omino**) is a [polyomino](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polyomino "Polyomino") of order 8; that is, a [polygon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polygon "Polygon") in the [plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plane_\(mathematics\) "Plane (mathematics)") made of 8 equal-sized [squares](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square_\(geometry\) "Square (geometry)") connected edge to edge.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Octomino#cite_note-1) When [rotations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotation "Rotation") and [reflections](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reflection_\(mathematics\) "Reflection (mathematics)") are not considered to be distinct shapes, there are [369](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/369_\(disambiguation\) "369 (disambiguation)") different [_free_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_polyomino "Free polyomino") octominoes. When reflections are considered distinct, there are 704 _one-sided_ octominoes. When rotations are also considered distinct, there are 2,725 _fixed_ octominoes.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Octomino#cite_note-2)[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Octomino#cite_note-Redelmeier-3)

## Symmetry

The figure shows all possible free octominoes, coloured according to their [symmetry groups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symmetry_group "Symmetry group"):

- 316 octominoes (coloured grey) have no [symmetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symmetry "Symmetry"). Their symmetry group consists only of the [identity mapping](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Identity_function "Identity function").
- 23 octominoes (coloured red) have an axis of [reflection symmetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reflection_symmetry "Reflection symmetry") aligned with the gridlines. Their symmetry group has two elements, the identity and the reflection in a line parallel to the sides of the squares.

[![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/Reflection_symmetrical_octominoes_90_deg.svg/250px-Reflection_symmetrical_octominoes_90_deg.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Reflection_symmetrical_octominoes_90_deg.svg)

- 5 octominoes (coloured green) have an axis of reflection symmetry at 45° to the gridlines. Their symmetry group has two elements, the identity and a diagonal reflection.

[![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/Reflection_symmetrical_octominoes_45_deg.svg/330px-Reflection_symmetrical_octominoes_45_deg.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Reflection_symmetrical_octominoes_45_deg.svg)

- 18 octominoes (coloured blue) have point symmetry, also known as [rotational symmetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotational_symmetry "Rotational symmetry") of order 2. Their symmetry group has two elements, the identity and the 180° rotation.

[![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/Rotation_symmetrical_octominoes_%28C2%29.svg/500px-Rotation_symmetrical_octominoes_%28C2%29.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Rotation_symmetrical_octominoes_\(C2\).svg)

- 1 octomino (coloured yellow) has rotational symmetry of order 4. Its symmetry group has four elements, the identity and the 90°, 180° and 270° rotations.

[![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Rotation_symmetrical_octomino_%28C4%29.svg/60px-Rotation_symmetrical_octomino_%28C4%29.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Rotation_symmetrical_octomino_\(C4\).svg)

- 4 octominoes (coloured purple) have two axes of reflection symmetry, both aligned with the gridlines. Their symmetry group has four elements, the identity, two reflections and the 180° rotation. It is the [dihedral group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dihedral_group "Dihedral group") of order 2, also known as the [Klein four-group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Klein_four-group "Klein four-group").
- 1 octomino (coloured orange) has two axes of reflection symmetry, both aligned with the diagonals. Its symmetry group is also the dihedral group of order 2 with four elements.
- 1 octomino (coloured cyan) has four axes of reflection symmetry, aligned with the gridlines and the diagonals, and rotational symmetry of order 4. Its symmetry group, the dihedral group of order 4, has eight elements.

[![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6e/Rotation_and_reflection_symmetric_octominoes_h.svg/330px-Rotation_and_reflection_symmetric_octominoes_h.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Rotation_and_reflection_symmetric_octominoes_h.svg)

The set of octominoes is the lowest polyomino set in which all eight possible symmetries are realized. The next higher set with this property is the dodecomino (12-omino) set.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Octomino#cite_note-Redelmeier-3)

If reflections of an octomino are considered distinct, as they are with one-sided octominoes, then the first, fourth and fifth categories above double in size, resulting in an extra 335 octominoes for a total of 704. If rotations are also considered distinct, then the octominoes from the first category count eightfold, the ones from the next three categories count fourfold, the ones from categories five to seven count twice, and the last octomino counts only once. This results in 316 × 8 + (23+5+18) × 4 + (1+4+1) × 2 + 1 = 2,725 fixed octominoes.

## Packing and tiling

Of the 369 free octominoes, 320 satisfy the [Conway criterion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conway_criterion "Conway criterion") and 23 more can form a patch satisfying the criterion.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Octomino#cite_note-4) The other 26 octominoes (including the 6 with holes) are unable to tessellate the plane.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Octomino#cite_note-5)

Since 6 of the free octominoes have a hole, it is trivial to prove that the complete set of octominoes cannot be [packed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Packing_problem "Packing problem") into a rectangle, and that not all octominoes can be [tiled](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tessellation "Tessellation").

When reflections are considered distinct, there are [196](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/196_\(number\) "196 (number)") _one-sided_ heptominoes. When rotations are also considered distinct, there are 760 _fixed_ heptominoes.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heptomino#cite_note-2)[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heptomino#cite_note-3)

## Packing and tiling

Of the 108 free heptominoes, 101 satisfy the [Conway criterion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conway_criterion "Conway criterion") and 3 more can form a patch satisfying the criterion. Only 4 heptominoes fail to satisfy the criterion and these 4 are unable to tessellate the plane.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heptomino#cite_note-4)

[![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/No_Tile_Heptominoes.png/500px-No_Tile_Heptominoes.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:No_Tile_Heptominoes.png)

The four heptominoes incapable of tiling a plane, including the one heptomino with a hole

Although a complete set of the 108 free heptominoes has a total of 756 squares, it is not possible to [tile](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tessellation "Tessellation") a [rectangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rectangle "Rectangle") with that set. The proof of this is trivial, since there is one heptomino which has a hole.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heptomino#cite_note-Gruenbaum-5) It is also impossible to form a 757-square rectangle because 757 is a prime number.

However, the set of 107 [simply connected](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simply_connected "Simply connected") free heptominoes—that is, the ones without the hole—can tile a 7 by 107 (749-square) rectangle.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heptomino#cite_note-6) Furthermore, the complete set of free heptominoes can tile three 11-by-23 (253-square) rectangles, each with a one-square hole in the center; the complete set can also tile twelve 8 by 8 (64-square) squares with a one-square hole in the "center".[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heptomino#cite_note-7)

Such a square containing 4,410 decominoes was constructed by Livio Zucca.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decomino#cite_note-3)

## Packing and tiling

Each of the 35 hexominoes satisfies the [Conway criterion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conway_criterion "Conway criterion"); hence, every hexomino is capable of tiling the plane.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hexomino#cite_note-4)

Although a complete set of 35 hexominoes has a total of 210 squares, it is not possible to pack them into a [rectangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rectangle "Rectangle"). (Such an arrangement is possible with the 12 [pentominoes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pentomino "Pentomino"), which can be packed into any of the rectangles 3 × 20, 4 × 15, 5 × 12 and 6 × 10.) A simple way to demonstrate that such a packing of hexominoes is not possible is via a [parity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parity_\(mathematics\) "Parity (mathematics)") argument. If the hexominoes are placed on a [checkerboard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Checkerboard "Checkerboard") pattern, then 11 of the hexominoes will cover an even number of black squares (either 2 white and 4 black or vice versa) and the other 24 hexominoes will cover an [odd number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Odd_number "Odd number") of black squares (3 white and 3 black). Overall, an even number of black squares will be covered in any arrangement. However, any rectangle of 210 squares will have 105 black squares and 105 white squares, and therefore cannot be covered by the 35 hexominoes.

However, there are other simple figures of 210 squares that can be packed with the hexominoes. For example, a 15 × 15 square with a 3 × 5 rectangle removed from the centre has 210 squares. With checkerboard colouring, it has 106 white and 104 black squares (or vice versa), so parity does not prevent a packing, and a packing is indeed possible.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hexomino#cite_note-5) It is also possible for two sets of pieces to fit a rectangle of size 420, or for the set of 60 one-sided hexominoes (18 of which cover an even number of black squares) to fit a rectangle of size 360.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hexomino#cite_note-6)

From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

[![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/aa/All_18_Pentominoes.svg/500px-All_18_Pentominoes.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:All_18_Pentominoes.svg)

The 12 pentominoes can form 18 different shapes, with 6 of them (the chiral pentominoes) being mirrored.

A **pentomino** (or **5-omino**) is a [polyomino](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polyomino "Polyomino") of order 5; that is, a [polygon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polygon "Polygon") in the [plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plane_\(geometry\) "Plane (geometry)") made of 5 equal-sized [squares](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square "Square") connected edge to edge. The term is derived from the Greek word for '[5](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/5 "5")' and "[domino](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Domino "Domino")". When [rotations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotation_symmetry "Rotation symmetry") and [reflections](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reflection_symmetry "Reflection symmetry") are not considered to be distinct shapes, there are 12 different _[free](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_polyomino "Free polyomino")_ pentominoes. When reflections are considered distinct, there are 18 _[one-sided](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/One-sided_polyomino "One-sided polyomino")_ pentominoes. When rotations are also considered distinct, there are 63 _[fixed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fixed_polyomino "Fixed polyomino")_ pentominoes.

Pentomino [tiling puzzles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tiling_puzzle "Tiling puzzle") and games are popular in [recreational mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Recreational_mathematics "Recreational mathematics").[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pentomino#cite_note-Harshbarger-1) Usually, [video games](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Video_game "Video game") such as _[Tetris](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetris "Tetris")_ imitations and [_Rampart_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rampart_\(game\) "Rampart (game)") consider mirror reflections to be distinct, and thus use the full set of 18 one-sided pentominoes. (Tetris itself uses 4-square shapes called [tetrominos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetromino "Tetromino").)

Each of the twelve pentominoes satisfies the [Conway criterion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conway_criterion "Conway criterion"); hence, every pentomino is capable of [tiling the plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tiling_the_plane "Tiling the plane").[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pentomino#cite_note-2) Each [chiral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chirality "Chirality") pentomino can tile the plane without being reflected.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pentomino#cite_note-3)

## History

[![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/69/Pentomino_Naming_Conventions.svg/500px-Pentomino_Naming_Conventions.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Pentomino_Naming_Conventions.svg)

Comparison of labeling schemes for the 12 possible pentomino shapes. The first naming convention is the one used in this article. The second method is Conway's.

The earliest puzzle containing a complete set of pentominoes appeared in [Henry Dudeney](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Henry_Dudeney "Henry Dudeney")'s book _[The Canterbury Puzzles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Canterbury_Puzzles "The Canterbury Puzzles")_, published in 1907.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pentomino#cite_note-4) The earliest tilings of rectangles with a complete set of pentominoes appeared in [the Problemist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Problemist "The Problemist") Fairy Chess Supplement in 1935, and further tiling problems were explored in the PFCS, and its successor, the [Fairy Chess Review](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fairy_Chess_Review "Fairy Chess Review").[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pentomino#cite_note-5)[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pentomino#cite_note-6): 127 

Pentominoes were formally defined by American professor [Solomon W. Golomb](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solomon_W._Golomb "Solomon W. Golomb") starting in 1953 and later in his 1965 book _[Polyominoes: Puzzles, Patterns, Problems, and Packings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polyominoes:_Puzzles,_Patterns,_Problems,_and_Packings "Polyominoes: Puzzles, Patterns, Problems, and Packings")_.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pentomino#cite_note-Harshbarger-1)[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pentomino#cite_note-7) They were introduced to the general public by [Martin Gardner](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Martin_Gardner "Martin Gardner") in his October 1965 [Mathematical Games column](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_Games_column "Mathematical Games column") in [Scientific American](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scientific_American "Scientific American"). Golomb coined the term "pentomino" from the [Ancient Greek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Greek "Ancient Greek") πέντε / _pénte_, "five", and the -omino of [domino](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Domino "Domino"), fancifully interpreting the "d-" of "domino" as if it were a form of the Greek prefix "di-" (two). Golomb named the 12 [_free_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_polyomino "Free polyomino") pentominoes after letters of the [Latin alphabet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latin_alphabet "Latin alphabet") that they resemble, using the [mnemonic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mnemonic "Mnemonic") FILiPiNo along with the end of the alphabet (TUVWXYZ).[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pentomino#cite_note-8): 23 

[John Horton Conway](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Horton_Conway "John Horton Conway") proposed an alternate labeling scheme for pentominoes, using O instead of I, Q instead of L, R instead of F, and S instead of N. The resemblance to the letters is more strained, especially for the O pentomino, but this scheme has the advantage of using 12 consecutive letters of the alphabet. It is used by convention in discussing [Conway's Game of Life](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conway%27s_Game_of_Life "Conway's Game of Life"), where, for example, one speaks of the R-pentomino instead of the F-pentomino.

## Symmetry

- F, L, N, P, and Y can be oriented in 8 ways: 4 by rotation, and 4 more for the mirror image. Their [symmetry group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symmetry_group "Symmetry group") consists only of the [identity mapping](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Identity_function "Identity function").
- T, and U can be oriented in 4 ways by rotation. They have an axis of [reflection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reflection_symmetry "Reflection symmetry") aligned with the gridlines. Their symmetry group has two elements, the identity and the reflection in a line parallel to the sides of the squares.
- V and W also can be oriented in 4 ways by rotation. They have an axis of reflection symmetry at 45° to the gridlines. Their symmetry group has two elements, the identity and a diagonal reflection.
- Z can be oriented in 4 ways: 2 by rotation, and 2 more for the mirror image. It has point symmetry, also known as [rotational symmetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotational_symmetry "Rotational symmetry") of order 2. Its symmetry group has two elements, the identity and the 180° rotation.
- I can be oriented in 2 ways by rotation. It has two axes of reflection symmetry, both aligned with the gridlines. Its symmetry group has four elements, the identity, two reflections and the 180° rotation. It is the [dihedral group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dihedral_group "Dihedral group") of order 2, also known as the [Klein four-group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Klein_four-group "Klein four-group").
- X can be oriented in only one way. It has four axes of reflection symmetry, aligned with the gridlines and the diagonals, and rotational symmetry of order 4. Its symmetry group, the dihedral group of order 4, has eight elements.

The F, L, N, P, Y, and Z pentominoes are [chiral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chirality_\(mathematics\) "Chirality (mathematics)"); adding their reflections (F′, J, N′, Q, Y′, S) brings the number of _one-sided_ pentominoes to 18. If rotations are also considered distinct, then the pentominoes from the first category count eightfold, the ones from the next three categories (T, U, V, W, Z) count fourfold, I counts twice, and X counts only once. This results in 5×8 + 5×4 + 2 + 1 = 63 _fixed_ pentominoes.

## Games

### Tiling puzzle (2D)

[![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Pentomino_Puzzle_Solutions.svg/500px-Pentomino_Puzzle_Solutions.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Pentomino_Puzzle_Solutions.svg)

Example tilings

A standard **pentomino puzzle** is to [tile](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tessellation "Tessellation") a rectangular box with the pentominoes, i.e. cover it without overlap and without gaps. Each of the 12 pentominoes has an area of 5 unit squares, so the box must have an area of 60 units. Possible sizes are 6×10, 5×12, 4×15 and 3×20.

The 6×10 case was first solved in 1960 by married couple [Colin Brian Haselgrove](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/C._Brian_Haselgrove "C. Brian Haselgrove") and [Jenifer Haselgrove](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jenifer_Haselgrove "Jenifer Haselgrove").[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pentomino#cite_note-9) There are exactly 2,339 solutions, excluding trivial variations obtained by rotation and reflection of the whole rectangle but including rotation and reflection of a subset of pentominoes (which sometimes provides an additional solution in a simple way). The 5×12 box has 1010 solutions, the 4×15 box has 368 solutions, and the 3×20 box has just 2 solutions (one is shown in the figure, and the other one can be obtained from the solution shown by rotating, as a whole, the block consisting of the L, N, F, T, W, Y, and Z pentominoes).

A somewhat easier (more symmetrical) puzzle, the 8×8 rectangle with a 2×2 hole in the center, was solved by [Dana Scott](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dana_Scott "Dana Scott") as far back as 1958.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pentomino#cite_note-10) There are 65 solutions. Scott's algorithm was one of the first applications of a [backtracking](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Backtracking "Backtracking") computer program. Variations of this puzzle allow the four holes to be placed in any position. One of the external links uses this rule.

Efficient algorithms have been described to solve such problems, for instance by [Donald Knuth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Donald_Knuth "Donald Knuth").[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pentomino#cite_note-11) Running on modern [hardware](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Personal_computer "Personal computer"), these pentomino puzzles can now be solved in mere seconds.

[![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/Pentomino_unsolvable.svg/500px-Pentomino_unsolvable.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Pentomino_unsolvable.svg)

Unsolvable patterns

Most such patterns are solvable, with the exceptions of placing each pair of holes near two corners of the board in such a way that both corners could only be fitted by a P-pentomino, or forcing a T-pentomino or U-pentomino in a corner such that another hole is created.

The pentomino set is the only free [polyomino](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polyomino "Polyomino") set that can be packed into a rectangle, with the exception of the trivial [monomino](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monomino "Monomino") and [domino](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Domino_\(mathematics\) "Domino (mathematics)") sets, each of which consists only of a single rectangle.

Each pentomino can be divided into nine pentominoes, as shown. [![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/1/1f/Triplication_of_pentaminoes.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Triplication_of_pentaminoes.png)[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

### Box filling puzzle (3D)

[![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/47/Pentomino_Cube_Solutions.svg/500px-Pentomino_Cube_Solutions.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Pentomino_Cube_Solutions.svg)

Sample solutions to pentacube puzzles of the stated dimensions, drawn one layer at a time.

A **pentacube** is a [polycube](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polycube "Polycube") of five cubes. Of the 29 one-sided pentacubes, exactly twelve pentacubes are flat (1-layer) and correspond to the twelve pentominoes extruded to a depth of one square.

A **pentacube puzzle** or 3D **pentomino puzzle**, amounts to filling a 3-dimensional box with the 12 flat pentacubes, i.e. cover it without overlap and without gaps. Since each pentacube has a volume of 5 unit cubes, the box must have a volume of 60 units. Possible sizes are 2×3×10 (12 solutions), 2×5×6 (264 solutions) and 3×4×5 (3940 solutions).[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pentomino#cite_note-12)

Alternatively one could also consider combinations of five cubes that are themselves 3D, i.e., those which include more than just the 12 "flat" single-layer thick combinations of cubes. However, in addition to the 12 "flat" [pentacubes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polycube "Polycube") formed by extruding the pentominoes, there are 6 sets of chiral pairs and 5 additional pieces, forming a total of 29 potential [pentacube](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polycube "Polycube") pieces, which gives 145 cubes in total (=29×5); as 145 can only be packed into a box measuring 29×5×1, it cannot be formed by including the non-flat pentominoes.

## Symmetry and enumeration

When [rotations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotation "Rotation") and [reflections](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reflection_\(mathematics\) "Reflection (mathematics)") are not considered to be distinct shapes, there are only two different [_free_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_polyomino "Free polyomino") trominoes: "I" and "L" (the "L" shape is also called "V").

When rotations are also considered distinct, there are six _fixed_ trominoes: two I and four L shapes. They can be obtained by rotating the above forms by 90°, 180° and 270°.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tromino#cite_note-2)[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tromino#cite_note-3)

A **pseudo-polyomino**, also called a **polyking**, **polyplet** or **hinged polyomino**, is a [plane geometric figure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shape "Shape") formed by joining one or more equal [squares](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square "Square") edge-to-edge or corner-to-corner at 90°. ### Free, one-sided, and fixed polykings

There are three common ways of distinguishing polyominoes and polykings for enumeration:[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pseudo-polyomino#cite_note-golomb1994-1)

- _free_ polykings are distinct when none is a rigid transformation ([translation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Translation_\(geometry\) "Translation (geometry)"), [rotation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotation "Rotation"), [reflection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reflection_\(mathematics\) "Reflection (mathematics)") or [glide reflection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glide_reflection "Glide reflection")) of another (pieces that can be picked up and flipped over).
- _one-sided_ polykings are distinct when none is a translation or rotation of another (pieces that cannot be flipped over).
- _fixed_ polykings are distinct when none is a translation of another (pieces that can be neither flipped nor rotated).

A **tetromino** is a geometric shape composed of four [squares](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square_\(geometry\) "Square (geometry)"), connected [orthogonally](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthogonality "Orthogonality") (i.e. at the edges and not the corners).[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetromino#cite_note-GolombPolyominoes-1)[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetromino#cite_note-2) Tetrominoes, like [dominoes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Domino_\(mathematics\) "Domino (mathematics)") and [pentominoes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pentomino "Pentomino"), are a particular type of [polyomino](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polyomino "Polyomino"). The corresponding [polycube](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polycube "Polycube"), called a **tetracube**, is a geometric shape composed of four [cubes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube "Cube") connected orthogonally.

A popular use of tetrominoes is in the video game _[Tetris](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetris "Tetris")_ created by the [Soviet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soviet "Soviet") game designer [Alexey Pajitnov](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alexey_Pajitnov "Alexey Pajitnov"), which refers to them as **tetriminos**.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetromino#cite_note-3) The tetrominoes used in the game are specifically the one-sided tetrominoes.

## Types of tetrominoes

### Free tetrominoes

Polyominos are formed by joining unit squares along their edges. A [free polyomino](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_polyomino "Free polyomino") is a polyomino considered up to [congruence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Congruence_\(geometry\) "Congruence (geometry)"). That is, two free polyominos are the same if there is a combination of [translations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Translation_\(geometry\) "Translation (geometry)"), [rotations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotation_\(mathematics\) "Rotation (mathematics)"), and [reflections](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reflection_\(mathematics\) "Reflection (mathematics)") that turns one into the other. A free tetromino is a free polyomino made from four squares. There are five free tetrominoes.

The free tetrominoes have the following symmetry:

- Straight: vertical and horizontal reflection symmetry, and two-fold rotational symmetry
- Square: vertical, horizontal, and diagonal reflection symmetry, and four-fold rotational symmetry
- T: vertical reflection symmetry only
- L: no symmetry
- S: two-fold rotational symmetry only

### One-sided tetrominoes

One-sided tetrominoes are tetrominoes that may be translated and rotated but not reflected. They are used by, and are overwhelmingly associated with, _[Tetris](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetris "Tetris")_. There are seven distinct one-sided tetrominoes. These tetrominoes are named by the letter of the alphabet they most closely resemble. The "I", "O", and "T" tetrominoes have reflectional symmetry, so it does not matter whether they are considered as free tetrominoes or one-sided tetrominoes. The remaining four tetrominoes, "J", "L", "S", and "Z", exhibit a phenomenon called [chirality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chirality_\(mathematics\) "Chirality (mathematics)"). J and L are reflections of each other, and S and Z are reflections of each other.

As free tetrominoes, J is equivalent to L, and S is equivalent to Z, but in two dimensions and without reflections, it is not possible to transform J into L or S into Z.

## Tiling a rectangle

### Filling a rectangle with one set of tetrominoes

A single set of free tetrominoes or one-sided tetrominoes cannot fit in a rectangle. This can be shown with a proof similar to the [mutilated chessboard argument](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mutilated_chessboard_problem "Mutilated chessboard problem"). A 5×4 rectangle with a checkerboard pattern has 20 squares, containing 10 light squares and 10 dark squares, but a complete set of free tetrominoes has either 11 dark squares and 9 light squares, or 11 light squares and 9 dark squares. This is due to the T tetromino having either 3 dark squares and one light square, or 3 light squares and one dark square, while all other tetrominoes each have 2 dark squares and 2 light squares. Similarly, a 7×4 rectangle has 28 squares, containing 14 squares of each shade, but the set of one-sided tetrominoes has either 15 dark squares and 13 light squares, or 15 light squares and 13 dark squares. By extension, any odd number of sets for either type cannot fit in a rectangle. Additionally, the 19 fixed tetrominoes cannot fit in a 4×19 rectangle. This was discovered by exhausting all possibilities in a computer search.

### Parity

A further consequence of the T tetromino having 3 squares of one colour and 1 square of the other is that any rectangle containing an even number of squares must contain an even number of T tetrominoes. Conversely, any rectangles containing an odd number of squares must contain an odd number of T tetrominoes.

- All 7 one-sided tetrominoes fit a 6×5 rectangle with two holes of the same "checkerboard color".
- All 19 fixed tetrominoes fit a 11×7 rectangle with a hole.

## Etymology

The name "tetromino" is a combination of the [prefix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prefix "Prefix") _tetra-_ 'four' (from [Ancient Greek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Greek "Ancient Greek") τετρα-), and "[domino](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Domino "Domino")". The name was introduced by [Solomon W. Golomb](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solomon_W._Golomb "Solomon W. Golomb") in 1953 along with other nomenclature related to polyominos.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetromino#cite_note-4)[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetromino#cite_note-GolombPolyominoes-1)

A **polycube** is an [orthogonal polyhedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthogonal_polyhedron "Orthogonal polyhedron") formed by joining one or more equal [cubes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube_\(geometry\) "Cube (geometry)") face to face. Polycubes are the three-dimensional analogues of the planar [polyominoes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polyomino "Polyomino"). The [Soma cube](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soma_cube "Soma cube"), the [Bedlam cube](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bedlam_cube "Bedlam cube"), the [Diabolical cube](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diabolical_cube "Diabolical cube"), the [Slothouber–Graatsma puzzle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Slothouber%E2%80%93Graatsma_puzzle "Slothouber–Graatsma puzzle"), and the [Conway puzzle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conway_puzzle "Conway puzzle") are examples of [packing problems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Packing_problem "Packing problem") based on polycubes.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polycube#cite_note-1)

## Enumerating polycubes

[![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cc/AGK-pentacube.png/250px-AGK-pentacube.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:AGK-pentacube.png)

A [chiral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chirality_\(mathematics\) "Chirality (mathematics)") pentacube

Like [polyominoes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polyomino "Polyomino"), polycubes can be enumerated in two ways, depending on whether [chiral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chirality_\(mathematics\) "Chirality (mathematics)") pairs of polycubes (those equivalent by [mirror reflection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reflection_symmetry "Reflection symmetry"), but not by using only translations and rotations) are counted as one polycube or two. For example, 6 tetracubes are achiral and one is chiral, giving a count of 7 or 8 tetracubes respectively.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polycube#cite_note-symmetry-2) Unlike polyominoes, polycubes are usually counted with mirror pairs distinguished, because one cannot turn a polycube over to reflect it as one can a polyomino given three dimensions. In particular, the [Soma cube](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soma_cube "Soma cube") uses both forms of the chiral tetracube.

Fixed polycubes (both reflections and rotations counted as distinct (sequence [A001931](https://oeis.org/A001931 "oeis:A001931") in the [OEIS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/On-Line_Encyclopedia_of_Integer_Sequences "On-Line Encyclopedia of Integer Sequences"))), one-sided polycubes, and free polycubes have been enumerated up to _n_=22. ## Symmetries of polycubes

As with polyominoes, polycubes may be classified according to how many symmetries they have. Polycube symmetries (conjugacy classes of subgroups of the achiral [octahedral group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Octahedral_group "Octahedral group")) were first enumerated by W. F. Lunnon in 1972. Most polycubes are asymmetric, but many have more complex symmetry groups, all the way up to the full symmetry group of the cube with 48 elements. There are 33 different symmetry types that a polycube can have (including asymmetry).[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polycube#cite_note-symmetry-2)

## Properties of pentacubes

12 pentacubes are flat and correspond to the [pentominoes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pentomino "Pentomino"). 5 of the remaining 17 have mirror symmetry, and the other 12 form 6 chiral pairs.

The bounding boxes of the pentacubes have sizes 5×1×1, 4×2×1, 3×3×1, 3×2×1, 3×2×2, and 2×2×2.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polycube#cite_note-6)

A polycube may have up to 24 orientations in the cubic lattice, or 48, if reflection is allowed. Of the pentacubes, 2 flats (5-1-1 and the cross) have mirror symmetry in all three axes; these have only three orientations. 10 have one mirror symmetry; these have 12 orientations. Each of the remaining 17 pentacubes has 24 orientations.

## Octacube and hypercube unfoldings

[![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/8-cell_net.png/250px-8-cell_net.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:8-cell_net.png)

The Dalí cross

The [tesseract](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tesseract "Tesseract") (four-dimensional [hypercube](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypercube "Hypercube")) has eight cubes as its [facets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Facet_\(geometry\) "Facet (geometry)"), and just as the cube can be [unfolded](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Net_\(polyhedron\) "Net (polyhedron)") into a [hexomino](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hexomino "Hexomino"), the tesseract can be unfolded into an octacube. One unfolding, in particular, mimics the well-known unfolding of a cube into a [Latin cross](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latin_cross "Latin cross"): it consists of four cubes stacked one on top of each other, with another four cubes attached to the exposed square faces of the second-from-top cube of the stack, to form a three-dimensional [double cross](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Two-barred_cross "Two-barred cross") shape. [Salvador Dalí](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Salvador_Dal%C3%AD "Salvador Dalí") used this shape in his 1954 painting _[Crucifixion (Corpus Hypercubus)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crucifixion_\(Corpus_Hypercubus\) "Crucifixion (Corpus Hypercubus)")_[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polycube#cite_note-7) and it is described in [Robert A. Heinlein](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_A._Heinlein "Robert A. Heinlein")'s 1940 short story "[And He Built a Crooked House](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/And_He_Built_a_Crooked_House "And He Built a Crooked House")".[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polycube#cite_note-8) In honor of Dalí, this octacube has been called the _Dalí cross_.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polycube#cite_note-hut-9)[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polycube#cite_note-pucc-10) It can [tile space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Honeycomb_\(geometry\) "Honeycomb (geometry)").[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polycube#cite_note-hut-9)

More generally (answering a question posed by [Martin Gardner](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Martin_Gardner "Martin Gardner") in 1966), out of all 3811 different free octacubes, 261 are unfoldings of the tesseract.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polycube#cite_note-hut-9)[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polycube#cite_note-11)

[![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1d/Distances_between_double_cube_corners.svg/250px-Distances_between_double_cube_corners.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Distances_between_double_cube_corners.svg)

Unlike in three dimensions in which distances between [vertices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vertex_\(geometry\) "Vertex (geometry)") of a polycube with unit edges excludes √7 due to [Legendre's three-square theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Legendre%27s_three-square_theorem "Legendre's three-square theorem"), [Lagrange's four-square theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lagrange%27s_four-square_theorem "Lagrange's four-square theorem") states that the analogue in four dimensions yields [square roots](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square_root "Square root") of every [natural number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_number "Natural number")

## Paradoxes

[![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Tangram_paradox_explanation.svg/250px-Tangram_paradox_explanation.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Tangram_paradox_explanation.svg)

Explanation of the two-monks paradox:  
In figure 1, side lengths are labelled assuming the square has unit sides.  
In figure 2, overlaying the bodies shows that footless body is larger by the foot's area. The change in area is often unnoticed as √2 is close to 1.5.

A tangram [paradox](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paradox "Paradox") is a dissection fallacy: Two figures composed with the same set of pieces, one of which seems to be a proper subset of the other.[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangram#cite_note-mathematica-21) One famous paradox is that of the two [monks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monk "Monk"), attributed to [Henry Dudeney](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Henry_Dudeney "Henry Dudeney"), which consists of two similar shapes, one with and the other missing a foot.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangram#cite_note-dudeney-22) In reality, the area of the foot is compensated for in the second figure by a subtly larger body.

The Magic Dice Cup tangram paradox – from [Sam Loyd](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sam_Loyd "Sam Loyd")'s book _The 8th Book of Tan_ (1903).[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangram#cite_note-eighth_book_1-23) Each of these cups was composed using the same seven geometric shapes. But the first cup is whole, and the others contain vacancies of different sizes. (Notice that the one on the left is slightly shorter than the other two. The one in the middle is ever-so-slightly wider than the one on the right, and the one on the left is narrower still.)[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangram#cite_note-24)

The set of _n_-sticks that contain no closed loops is equivalent, with some duplications, to the set of [(_n_+1)-ominos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polyomino "Polyomino"), as each [vertex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vertex_\(graph_theory\) "Vertex (graph theory)") at the end of every line segment can be replaced with a single square of a polyomino. For example, the set of tristicks is equivalent to the set of [Tetrominos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetrominos "Tetrominos"). In general, an _n_-stick with _m_ loops is equivalent to a (_n_−_m_+1)-omino (as each loop means that one line segment does not add a vertex to the figure).
